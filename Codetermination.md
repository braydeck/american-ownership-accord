**THE AMERICAN OWNERSHIP ECONOMY**

A Codetermination and Worker Equity Framework

Integrated with the American Ownership Accord

**WORKING DRAFT**

# **Executive Summary**

The American Ownership Economy shifts the legal basis of worker economic rights from a regulatory framework to a property framework. Prior codetermination proposals — including mandatory board representation statutes — rely on government regulation that can be repealed by a subsequent Congress, interpreted narrowly by hostile courts, and bypassed through corporate restructuring. This framework operates differently: it legally transfers a defined percentage of enterprise value directly to workers, converting wage-dependents into property-holding owners whose interests are aligned with long-term corporate value creation rather than opposed to it.

The system is stratified into four tiers based on Enterprise Value and integrates directly with the 0% Corporate Income Tax and the EV Growth Tax described in the American Ownership Accord. It is designed to provide universal worker wealth and governance participation across all enterprise scales without creating administrative deadweight, destroying operating liquidity for small businesses, or forcing private companies into public markets before they are ready.

The framework formally separates governance power from individual wealth creation, recognizing that these serve different functions and require different institutional structures. Workers receive both: a Stewardship Trust that cannot be sold but exercises permanent voting leverage, and a Phantom Equity Fund that accumulates individual wealth and pays out upon departure.

# **The Core Philosophy: Property Over Regulation**

The American legal and economic system is structurally biased toward the enforcement of property rights over the enforcement of regulatory mandates. A statutory right to a board seat can be repealed. A property right in a fractional ownership stake cannot be taken without compensation. This distinction is not merely philosophical — it determines the durability of any reform under legal challenge, political reversal, and corporate resistance.

Capital dictates corporate behavior. Rather than regulating how concentrated capital is managed — an approach that invites adversarial dynamics and enforcement costs — this framework legally dilutes concentrated capital at the source. The ownership stake transferred to workers is not a benefit granted by management; it is a property right vested by federal law as a condition of operating under the legal privileges the state confers. Workers are co-owners from the moment of vesting.

This framing aligns the American ethos of individual wealth-building with the labor priority of collective job security. A worker who owns 0.01% of a $10 billion company has the same interest in that company's long-term health as its largest institutional investors. The system creates this alignment structurally, not aspirationally.

# **Part I: The Two-Pocket Mandate**

Every corporation with Enterprise Value above $100 million is required to establish two linked but functionally distinct structures for its non-executive workforce. The two pockets are intentionally separated: one holds governance power with no economic value; the other holds economic value with no governance power. This separation prevents the concentration of both forms of leverage in a single structure that could be captured, diluted, or negotiated away.

## **Pocket 1: The Stewardship Trust — Power**

The Stewardship Trust secures institutional leverage and strategic veto power for the workforce on a permanent basis.

**The Asset:** The company issues a distinct class of Stewardship Shares to a democratically elected Worker Trust.

**Voting Weight:** Stewardship Shares carry a floating voting weight permanently fixed at 20% of the total board vote, regardless of any future stock issuance, dilution, or capital structure change by founders, investors, or management.

**Economic Value:** $0. Stewardship Shares pay no dividends and cannot be sold, liquidated, pledged, or transferred. They exist solely as a governance instrument.

The economic nullity of Stewardship Shares is a deliberate design feature, not a limitation. It prevents the voting bloc from being bought out, undermined through dilution, or concentrated through secondary market accumulation. The 20% governance stake is permanent and inalienable as long as the company operates.

### **The Shadow Board — Information Parity**

Worker voting power is only meaningful if workers have access to the information required to use it. The most common failure mode in European codetermination models is information asymmetry: management controls what the worker representatives see, and representatives are reduced to approving decisions they cannot meaningfully evaluate.

The Shadow Board addresses this structurally. A select committee of workers — elected by the broader worker trust — signs strict non-disclosure agreements and receives full access to confidential corporate financials, strategic plans, and board materials alongside the Trust's elected board representatives. Shadow Board members review the same documents management reviews. Worker board votes are cast with full informational parity, not on the basis of management summaries.

## **Pocket 2: The Phantom Equity Fund — Wealth**

The Phantom Equity Fund provides the actual financial payout to workers, functioning as a structurally protected profit-sharing plan tied directly to the long-term value of the enterprise.

**The Asset:** A pool of Phantom Stock Units (PSUs) or real equity equivalent to 20% of the company's total valuation at equilibrium.

**Funding Mechanism:** The company is assessed an annual Equity Excise Tax of 4% of its Enterprise Value. The tax is satisfied by issuing PSUs into the worker fund until the pool reaches the 20% equilibrium cap (approximately five years), at which point the annual tax drops to 0%.

### **Payouts**

* Dividends: When the company pays dividends to standard shareholders, it must simultaneously issue an equivalent proportional cash payout to all PSU holders. Workers receive the same per-unit cash flow as institutional investors.

* Exit: When a worker quits or retires, they surrender their PSUs. The company is legally required to redeem those units at their current fair market valuation in cash, out of operating cash flow. Redemptions exceeding the annual cash flow cap are satisfied via structured promissory notes (see Part V: Firewalls).

  *The Equity Excise Tax is structured as a deductible component of the EV Growth Tax — a single federal excise on the privilege of corporate operation (Accord Section 1.3). The 4% annual worker equity transfer generates Codetermination Credits equal to its full value. These credits may offset up to 20% of EV Growth Tax liability in any given year and carry forward indefinitely. This means the AMCF receives at least 80% of growth tax revenue from Day 1, even during the five-year worker equity ramp when credit generation exceeds the current year's Growth Tax. The credit structure mirrors established tax law (Section 41 R\&D Credit, Section 904 Foreign Tax Credit) and reinforces the constitutional framing: the codetermination obligation is a method of partial satisfaction of the excise, not an independent regulatory mandate. The Supreme Court's doctrine since Flint v. Stone Tracy (1911) permits Congress to condition the privilege of operating in corporate form on payment of an excise measured by business value. The codetermination framework must never be described in statute, committee reports, or legislative history as a labor regulation, property mandate, or standalone ownership requirement — it is a tax credit with carryforward, satisfied through equity allocation.*

# **Part II: The Stratified Market Protocol**

A strict $100 million Enterprise Value threshold would exclude approximately 50% of the US private-sector workforce employed by smaller businesses. Applying the full Two-Pocket Mandate to micro-enterprises and small businesses would impose administrative costs and equity dilution that would be economically destructive for low-margin local operators. The Stratified Protocol solves this by scaling both the funding mechanism and the governance structure to the actual capacity of each enterprise tier.

## **Tier 1: Micro-Enterprise ($0 to $10 Million EV)**

**Tax Status:** Exempt from the EV Growth Tax and all corporate Sectoral Fund cash levies.

**Worker Wealth:** The federal Treasury deposits a $1,000 annual grant directly into each worker's Sectoral Wealth Fund account, funded by the broader revenue base (VAT, LVT, payroll tax). Total cost approximately $27 billion annually.

### **Statutory Downstream Governance**

Micro-enterprises lack formal boards of directors, making the full Two-Pocket Mandate inapplicable. Governance adapts to the actual structure of these businesses:

* Any enterprise employing 5 or more people must allow the election of a Worker Representative by majority vote of non-owner employees.

* The Worker Representative has statutory rights to review the owner's financial books on a quarterly basis.

* The Worker Representative must be formally consulted — with documented notice and a defined response window — prior to any major operational shifts: layoffs exceeding 20% of headcount, relocation of primary operations, significant changes to compensation structure, or sale of the business.

* Consultation rights do not include veto power. The obligation is to inform and consider, not to obtain consent. This is the appropriate governance scope for enterprises at this scale.

## **Tier 2: Traditional Mid-Market ($10M to $100M EV)**

### **The Main Street Safe Harbor**

Private mid-market firms face a valuation problem: accurate enterprise value requires expensive 409A appraisals or formal accounting processes that are cost-prohibitive for closely-held businesses. The Federal Valuation Office (Accord Section 2.5) eliminates this friction by providing business enterprise valuations at no cost to the entity. Tier 2 firms establish their taxable EV through the Accord's mandatory reporting hierarchy — LP Disclosure Peg, QLE Safe Harbor, or free FVO appraisal — without needing to fund their own valuation process.

### **Worker Equity — Sectoral Wealth Fund Equivalence**

Tier 2 firms owe the same 20% worker equity obligation as Tier 4 corporations, but satisfy it through Sectoral Wealth Fund contributions rather than an internally managed Phantom Equity Fund. The Sectoral Wealth Fund is an externally administered, pooled investment vehicle — eliminating the administrative burden of maintaining individual PSU accounts, managing redemptions, and tracking vesting for mid-market firms that lack the back-office infrastructure of large corporations. The annual Equity Excise Tax of 4% of enterprise value applies identically to Tier 2, with contributions flowing to the Sectoral Fund until the cumulative worker equity position reaches the 20% equilibrium cap (approximately five years). Workers receive equivalent economic exposure to the employer's enterprise value growth through the Sectoral Fund's holdings, and equivalent dividend participation when the employer pays distributions to shareholders.

### **Worker Governance — Stewardship Trust**

Tier 2 firms must establish a 20% Stewardship Trust voting bloc on the same terms as Tier 4 corporations. The governance structure applies universally above the $10M exemption threshold. For firms with a formal board of directors, the Trust operates identically to Tier 4. For closely-held firms without a formal board, the Stewardship Trust voting bloc attaches to all major governance decisions requiring owner approval — including sale of the business, merger, liquidation, executive compensation above a statutory threshold, and material changes to the capital structure. The Shadow Board information-parity provisions (Part I) apply at Tier 2.

### **The Closely-Held Cash Election**

Mid-market firms owe the EV Growth Tax in addition to the Equity Excise Tax. Firms may satisfy the EV Growth Tax through scrip issuance (the standard mechanism) or may elect to pay in cash. The Closely-Held Cash Election is an option, not a mandate — firms that prefer to issue scrip to the AMCF may do so. For firms that elect cash, the AMCF uses the proceeds to purchase equity on the open market consistent with its indexed passive investment mandate (Accord Section 1.4), ensuring that cash payments build the same compounding equity base as scrip.

## **Tier 3: The Venture Exception**

**Target:** Unprofitable but high-valuation private firms — startups that generate no free cash flow but hold substantial paper valuations funded by LP capital.

**Valuation Peg:** EV is strictly pegged to the highest NAV or FMV reported to any Limited Partner or investor during the fiscal year, via the LP Disclosure Peg mechanism described in the Accord (Accord Section 2.1). All LP-reported valuations must be stated on a common-stock-equivalent basis, stripping out the economic value of liquidation preferences, participation rights, and other senior security protections — ensuring that workers' Phantom Equity is calculated against the actual value of common shares, not the inflated headline valuation that reflects investor preference stacks. For entities funded exclusively through unpriced convertible instruments (SAFEs, convertible notes), the Convertible Instrument Floor (Accord Section 2.1) establishes a minimum taxable EV base.

**Funding Mechanism:** Because these firms are cash-poor, they cannot use the Closely-Held Cash Election. They satisfy their Sectoral Fund liability by issuing private equity scrip directly proportional to their declared NAV growth. Workers receive equity stakes in firms at the stage when that equity is most valuable — at pre-exit valuation, not post-IPO dilution.

## **Tier 4: Corporate ($100M+ EV)**

**Target:** Publicly traded companies and mega-cap private firms.

**Funding Mechanism:** Subject to the full EV Growth Tax (payable in scrip to the AMCF) and legally mandated to implement the complete Two-Pocket Mandate: 20% Phantom Equity Fund plus 20% Stewardship Trust voting bloc.

# **Part III: The Living System — Recycling Loop**

A static equity allocation creates a worker aristocracy problem: early employees accumulate large PSU positions and then rationally oppose expansion that would dilute their per-unit value, even if that expansion increases the absolute dollar value of the pool. The Recycling Loop prevents this by making the worker equity system dynamic and self-correcting.

**The Reservoir:** The 20% Phantom Equity pool is a permanent equilibrium cap, not a one-time transfer. Once the cap is reached, the 4% annual Equity Excise Tax drops to 0%.

**The Outflow:** When a worker retires or departs, they surrender their PSUs and receive cash redemption. Surrendered units are canceled or returned to the company treasury, dropping the worker pool below 20%.

**The Refill:** The drop below equilibrium automatically reactivates the Equity Excise Tax. New PSUs are issued and granted to new or continuing employees until the 20% level is restored.

This mechanism produces a powerful alignment of incentives: existing workers benefit from expansion only if it drives genuine enterprise value growth. If a company hires 1,000 new workers, the existing workers' per-unit value dilutes unless the company's overall valuation increases proportionally. Workers will approve expansions that create value and oppose those that destroy it — which is exactly the governance signal that productive boards should be receiving.

# **Part IV: The Tech Sector Protocol**

The technology sector will argue that existing Restricted Stock Unit (RSU) programs already provide meaningful worker ownership. This argument is empirically false and must be addressed directly in any legislative defense of the framework.

The structural reality of tech equity distribution is that named executives and senior management consume 60% to 70% of the total equity pool. The 95% rank-and-file receive approximately 3% to 5% of actual company ownership. These RSUs are subject to continuous dilution as new stock is issued, carry no voting rights, and are allocated at the discretion of management. They are compensation instruments, not ownership instruments.

## **The Four Corrections**

**C-Suite Exclusion:** Named executive officers and all upper management are legally excluded from the 20% Phantom Equity pool and the Stewardship Trust. The wealth and power structures exist exclusively for the non-executive labor force. This is not punitive toward executives — they continue to receive equity compensation through existing mechanisms — but it ensures the worker ownership structures serve their intended function.

**Anti-Dilution Floor:** Existing RSUs lose proportional value whenever new stock is issued. The Phantom pool, by contrast, is pegged to a hard 20% of total company value. Any new stock issuance — including executive compensation grants, secondary offerings, and acquisition currency — automatically triggers a top-off requirement to restore the 20% equilibrium. Worker ownership cannot be diluted.

**The Qualified Equity Credit:** Companies with existing broad-based RSU programs can credit the dollar value of those grants against their 4% Equity Excise Tax liability. However, because current non-executive RSU distributions rarely approach 20% of total company value, virtually all tech firms will be required to dramatically increase rank-and-file equity grants to satisfy the tax. The credit is a bridge, not an exemption.

**The Trust Mandate Remains:** Even if a company fully satisfies the Phantom Equity pocket through existing RSU programs, the Stewardship Trust mandate is not waived. The 20% voting bloc must be established separately and independently. Founder super-voting share structures — dual-class shares with 10:1 or 20:1 voting ratios — are subject to dilution by the Stewardship Trust's floating 20% of total board vote, which operates as a permanent floor regardless of the capital structure above it.

# **Part V: Legal and Economic Firewalls**

## **Constitutional Defense — Excise, Not Expropriation**

The codetermination framework's constitutional durability rests on its integration with the EV Growth Tax as a single federal excise on the privilege of corporate operation. The Equity Excise Tax is not a separate tax — it is a credit-generating component of the EV Growth Tax. The Accord (Section 1.3) provides that the 4% annual equity transfer to the Worker Trust generates Codetermination Credits equal to its full value, offsetting up to 20% of EV Growth Tax liability in any given year and carrying forward indefinitely. This means there is one excise, measured by one metric (enterprise value), with the codetermination obligation functioning as a method of partial satisfaction — analogous to how the R\&D tax credit reduces income tax liability without creating a separate "research tax." The credit structure — with annual caps, indefinite carryforward, and percentage-of-liability limitations — is standard Internal Revenue Code architecture (Section 41 R\&D Credit, Section 904 Foreign Tax Credit), further reinforcing the tax characterization.

This unified framing is constitutionally critical. The Supreme Court established in Flint v. Stone Tracy Co. (1911) that Congress may condition the privilege of operating in corporate form on payment of an excise measured by business value. A single excise with a worker-equity deduction fits squarely within this doctrine. By contrast, framing the codetermination obligation as an independent regulatory mandate — separate from the tax — would invite Takings analysis under Cedar Point Nursery v. Hassid (2021), where the Court held that government-compelled physical appropriation of private property triggers per se Taking scrutiny.

The defense therefore requires that every aspect of the codetermination framework — Stewardship Shares, Phantom Stock Units, Sectoral Fund contributions — be drafted, titled, and supported by legislative history as incidents of the EV Growth Tax excise satisfied through equity allocation, not as standalone labor regulations or property mandates. No statute, committee report, or administration statement should ever describe the mandate as a property transfer, an ownership requirement, or a labor regulation.

Under Pennell v. City of San Jose (1988), economic regulation affecting property value does not automatically constitute a Taking. Nollan/Dolan nexus and proportionality tests apply to permit conditions, not to excise taxes — so any statutory language or legislative history that frames the codetermination mandate as a regulatory labor requirement rather than a tax incident creates constitutional vulnerability that must be avoided.

**⚑ Red Team Defense — Takings Clause:** If a constitutional challenge characterizes the Stewardship Share issuance as a per se physical appropriation under Cedar Point, the strongest response is the franchise conditions doctrine: conditions on the privilege of operating in corporate form are not Takings, they are terms of the franchise. The state created the corporation; the state may impose excise conditions on its operation. This is analogous to requiring incorporation fees, securities disclosure, or annual franchise taxes — none of which are Takings. The unified single-excise framing — where worker equity is a deductible satisfaction method rather than an independent obligation — strengthens this defense by eliminating the appearance of a separate regulatory mandate.

## **Preventing Capital Flight**

Multinational corporations operating in the United States must do so through a US-incorporated subsidiary (Accord Section 2.6). That subsidiary is subject to the EV Growth Tax and, above the $10M exemption threshold, the codetermination obligations appropriate to its tier — including the 20% Stewardship Trust voting bloc, which applies universally above $10M. The Federal Valuation Office (Accord Section 2.5) appraises US subsidiaries of foreign parents based on the actual operations, assets, revenue, and workforce of the US entity — not on the foreign parent's internal transfer pricing allocations.

US-incorporated entities that redomicile to a foreign jurisdiction but retain substantial US operations are treated as domestic entities for EV Growth Tax and codetermination purposes under the Anti-Inversion Provision (Accord Section 2.6), which triggers if 25% or more of the entity's workforce, tangible assets, or gross revenue remains US-based following the redomiciliation. Firms that attempt corporate inversion or operational expatriation to avoid the Two-Pocket Mandate trigger an immediate Expatriation Tax on the total fair market value of US assets. The cost of exit is designed to exceed the capitalized present value of the avoided mandate.

## **Preventing the Run on the Bank**

If multiple senior employees retire simultaneously — a coordination problem that could theoretically be engineered to drain corporate cash — PSU redemptions are capped at 5% of the company's free cash flow per fiscal year. Any redemption amount exceeding this cap is satisfied via a 5-year promissory note bearing mandated interest at the Treasury rate plus 2%. This converts a potential liquidity crisis into a structured debt obligation, protecting operating cash flow while fully honoring the worker's property right in their PSU value.

## **Eliminating the Threshold Effect**

The progressive tier structure creates an incentive to artificially suppress Enterprise Value below tier boundaries. The IRS Controlled Group aggregation rules are applied with full force: any network of entities sharing common ownership above 50%, common management, or common customers representing more than 30% of revenue is treated as a single enterprise for purposes of EV calculation and tier classification. The financial incentive to fragment operations into shell LLCs is eliminated.

## **Expanding and Federalizing Fiduciary Duty**

State corporate law — most critically Delaware's Revlon doctrine, which requires boards to maximize short-term shareholder value in sale contexts — is preempted by a Federal Constituency Statute. Stewardship Trust representatives owe a fiduciary duty to the long-term economic stability and employment interests of the workforce, explicitly defined in federal statute as taking precedence over short-term share price maximization. Representatives acting consistently with this duty are immunized from minority shareholder litigation.

The Federal Constituency Statute also preempts state anti-takeover provisions that could be used to nullify the Stewardship Trust's governance role. The 20% voting bloc is a federal property right; state law cannot dilute it.

# **Integration with the American Ownership Accord**

The American Ownership Economy codetermination framework and the American Ownership Accord tax structure are designed as a single integrated system. Neither works at full effectiveness without the other.

The 0% Corporate Income Tax is not a gift to shareholders — it is the operating cash flow release that funds Tier 2 Closely-Held Cash Elections and makes the Equity Excise Tax cost-neutral for mid-market businesses. The EV Growth Tax scrip mechanism is not merely a revenue instrument — it is the equity transfer pipeline that builds the AMCF's passive ownership stake while simultaneously forcing dividend behavior that generates cash distributions for both the AMCF and worker Phantom Equity holders. The universal prebate is not a welfare program — it is the universal floor that allows the consumption tax to be applied at a flat rate without regressivity, which in turn generates the revenue base that funds Priority 1 social infrastructure.

The two documents should be read together and, if enacted, drafted as a single legislative package. The constitutional defenses, valuation mechanisms, aggregation rules, and revenue flows are interdependent. Separating them for political convenience risks undermining the structural integrity that makes each element defensible.

*The Ownership Economy framework formally separates governance leverage from individual wealth creation, ensuring workers capture both the capital gains of their labor and the structural power to defend it — across all enterprise scales, from the corner restaurant to the multinational corporation.*
