# **Executive Summary**

The American Ownership Accord is a unified fiscal and ownership reform designed to accomplish four interdependent goals over a twenty-year horizon: eliminate the structural distortions of the current corporate tax code, build a sovereign wealth fund on behalf of all Americans through passive equity accumulation, replace the welfare cliff of means-tested benefits with a universal income floor, and measure national progress through the American Commonwealth Index — a composite metric that makes government performance legible to every citizen-owner.

The Accord is not a short-term deficit reduction plan. It is a sovereign balance sheet strategy. The federal government will borrow at Treasury rates to acquire American corporate equity at market rates, and the long-run spread between compounding equity returns and debt service cost is the engine of fiscal consolidation. This framing must be understood and accepted at the outset: the national debt will continue growing through approximately Year 13 to 15 before stabilizing, and the deleveraging phase begins in earnest only after the American Capital Fund reaches sufficient scale to generate distributable cash exceeding the structural deficit.

The fiscal crossover — the point at which AMCF dividend income structurally exceeds the shrinking General Fund deficit — is projected between Years 14 and 18 under conservative assumptions, accelerated by healthcare cost reform and immigration stabilization. Aggressive deleveraging and the Priority 3 discretionary unlock follow in Years 18 to 25\.

Every American is a citizen-owner of the Accord's outputs: a universal prebate income floor, an annual AMCF equity grant that compounds from birth, and a national wellbeing dashboard — the American Commonwealth Index — that makes the connection between good governance and personal financial outcomes tangible and direct.

# **Part 1: The Revenue Engine — The Grand Swap**

The core corporate taxation reform eliminates profit-based taxation in favor of a levy on enterprise value growth. This structural shift eliminates the primary mechanisms of tax avoidance — profit shifting, earnings management, and debt-financed suppression of taxable equity — while preserving full operating liquidity for productive reinvestment.

## **1.1 The EV Growth Tax**

Tax Rate: 20% on the increase in Enterprise Value (EV), calculated via a rolling 3-year high-water mark.

Enterprise Value is determined through mandatory reporting, with the method varying by entity type:

* Publicly traded entities: EV is calculated from observed market data — market capitalization of all outstanding equity securities plus total debt minus cash and cash equivalents — using the fiscal year-end closing price. The taxable base is the change in this observed EV, subject to the high-water mark. No additional reporting is required; the IRS computes EV from existing SEC filings.

* Private entities above the $10M exemption threshold: EV must be established annually through one of three mechanisms — (a) the LP Disclosure Peg (Section 2.1), using the highest investor-reported valuation on a common-stock-equivalent basis; (b) the QLE Safe Harbor (Section 2.2), using empirical pricing from an arm's-length transaction conducted at least once every two years; or (c) a valuation conducted by the Federal Valuation Office (Section 2.5), an independent statutory agency that provides business enterprise valuations at no cost to the entity. The FVO valuation is the default for any private business without investor reporting or a recent qualifying transaction; entities that disagree may challenge the FVO determination with their own Qualified Independent Appraisal at their own expense.

* Non-compliance: Entities that fail to report are subject to the Non-Compliance Penalty (Section 2.3). The penalty is assessed upon eventual determination of EV through IRS audit, voluntary disclosure, or a qualifying transaction, and is designed to make non-reporting strictly more expensive than any of the three reporting options above.

Taxing Enterprise Value rather than Market Capitalization provides capital structure neutrality. A firm cannot suppress its taxable base by over-leveraging with private credit, because debt is additive to EV rather than deductive. This closes the most aggressively exploited gap in current corporate taxation.

* Definition of Debt: For purposes of the EV formula, "debt" includes all interest-bearing financial obligations — corporate bonds, term loans, revolving credit facilities, interbank borrowings, repurchase agreements, commercial paper, and subordinated notes. It excludes customer deposits held by regulated depository institutions, policyholder reserves and claim liabilities held by regulated insurance companies, client funds held in fiduciary or custodial capacity by regulated broker-dealers, any other liability subject to federal or state prudential capital requirements, and structured promissory notes issued to satisfy Phantom Stock Unit (PSU) redemptions under the codetermination framework. The excluded liabilities share a common characteristic: they are either the core product or operating obligation of a regulated financial business, or a statutory obligation arising from the Accord's own worker equity architecture — not discretionary financing choices. This ensures a single EV formula applies uniformly to all entities — financial and non-financial — without sector-specific carve-outs, while preventing the formula from producing inflated enterprise values through liabilities that are regulatory or statutory in origin rather than capital-structure decisions.

## **1.2 Payment Mechanism — Corporate Scrip Escrow**

* Tax liability is satisfied through issuance of new corporate scrip into a 3-Year Sustained Accrual Escrow. The scrip represents a share of new value created (EV growth), not a transfer of existing ownership — existing shareholders retain their full claim on the company's pre-growth enterprise value. The AMCF receives equity proportional to 20% of the value increase, and existing shareholders retain 80% of all new value created plus 100% of pre-existing value.

* Shares vest to the American Capital Fund (AMCF) at 33% per year over three years.

* Scrip vests unconditionally once issued. If a company's valuation declines below the high-water mark during or after the vesting window, the AMCF absorbs the downside as an equity holder alongside all other shareholders. The high-water mark (Section 1.1) ensures no additional tax is assessed until the prior peak is exceeded, but previously issued scrip is not canceled. This mirrors standard tax finality — realized tax obligations are not refunded when subsequent conditions change — and eliminates the gaming incentive that a conditional vesting structure would create.

*Constitutional framing: The EV Growth Tax is structured as a federal excise on the privilege of operating as a juridical person under state and federal law, measured by the appreciation in that entity's enterprise value. This mirrors the corporate excise upheld in Flint v. Stone Tracy Co. (1911). For publicly traded entities, the taxable base is derived from observable, market-determined prices. For private entities, the taxable base is established through the entity's own reporting — investor disclosures, arm's-length transactions, or independent appraisals — ensuring that the government taxes a value the entity itself has affirmed. The scrip payment mechanism avoids realization problems by compelling a fractional ownership transfer rather than demanding cash on unrealized gains.*

## **1.3 The Grand Swap**

* The Corporate Income Tax (CIT) is reduced to 0%. The interest deduction for corporate debt is simultaneously abolished. This neutralizes the debt-over-equity bias embedded in current law and eliminates the single most common avoidance structure.

* Codetermination Deduction: A statutory 4% annual equity transfer to the Worker Trust generates Codetermination Credits equal to the full value of the transfer. The 4% rate achieves the 20% worker equity equilibrium cap within five years — fast enough to deliver meaningful worker ownership within a single business cycle, while preserving the annual excise framing required for constitutional defensibility.

* Codetermination Credit Application: Accumulated Codetermination Credits may offset up to 20% of EV Growth Tax liability in any given year and carry forward indefinitely. This means the AMCF receives at least 80% of the EV Growth Tax in every year, including during the five-year worker equity ramp when the Equity Excise generates credits far exceeding the current year's Growth Tax. The excess credits accumulate and are drawn down over approximately 15 to 20 years following the ramp. This structure mirrors established tax credit mechanics (Section 41 R\&D Credit, Section 904 Foreign Tax Credit) and produces a superior AMCF accumulation trajectory compared to a full current-year deduction: the fund collects 80% of growth tax revenue from Year 1 rather than zero during the ramp, generating approximately $4 trillion in additional early-phase equity that compounds for the fund's full lifetime.

* Aggregation Rules: IRS Controlled Group Aggregation applies across all entities sharing common ownership or management chains, preventing artificial fragmentation into sub-threshold shell companies.

## **1.4 The Stabilization Bridge — Treasury SPV**

During Phase 1 (Years 1 through approximately 6), the EV Growth Tax produces scrip rather than cash. A liquidity bridge is required to fund operating needs while the AMCF's organic dividend yield scales up.

* The Treasury establishes a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV) capitalized by a defined tranche of Treasury bond issuance.

* The SPV purchases incoming corporate scrip at a discount, providing liquid cash to the General Fund in exchange for equity positions that will appreciate as the AMCF matures.

* The SPV's discount rate creates a market discipline mechanism: scrip from high-quality issuers trades at smaller discounts, incentivizing corporate governance from Day 1\.

* As the AMCF's organic dividend yield reaches self-sustaining levels by Year 6 to 8, the SPV unwinds. Treasury redeems its bond tranche using AMCF distributions, and the government retains the equity.

* Cash Election Proceeds: When private entities satisfy the EV Growth Tax through the Closely-Held Cash Election rather than scrip issuance, the cash proceeds flow to the AMCF, which deploys them to purchase equity on the open market — indexed passive acquisitions consistent with the AMCF's investment mandate (Section 5.2). This ensures that cash payments from private companies build the same compounding equity base as scrip payments from public companies, preserving the AMCF's long-term accumulation trajectory regardless of the payment mechanism used.

**⚠ Red Team Defense —** *Dividend Yield Behavior: By abolishing the 21% effective tax penalty on distributed corporate profits and installing a Worker Trust that structurally demands cash payouts over buybacks, market dividend yields are projected to rise from their current 1.5% average toward 3% to 5%. At that yield on a growing AMCF equity position, the Fund extracts hundreds of billions in liquid cash annually without liquidating its compounding principal.*

# **Part 2: The Private Market Protocol**

Non-traded entities are brought into the EV Growth Tax base through mandatory valuation reporting. Every private entity above the $10M exemption threshold must establish its taxable Enterprise Value annually through investor disclosures, arm's-length transactions, or independent appraisals. The protocol is designed so that compliance is simple and inexpensive relative to the penalties for non-reporting.

## **2.1 The LP Disclosure Peg**

For any private firm, the taxable EV base is strictly pegged to the highest Net Asset Value (NAV) or Fair Market Value (FMV) reported to any Limited Partner, creditor, or investor during the fiscal year. This eliminates the need for a separate government valuation apparatus.

* Common Stock Equivalent Requirement: All LP-reported valuations must be stated on a common-stock-equivalent basis, stripping out the economic value of liquidation preferences, participation rights, anti-dilution ratchets, and other senior security protections. The required methodology is the Option Pricing Model (OPM) backsolve, using equity volatility assumptions derived from a published index of comparable public company volatilities maintained by the Federal Valuation Office (Section 2.5). By standardizing the volatility input, the OPM output becomes replicable across valuation firms — eliminating the judgment divergence that would otherwise produce materially different common-equivalent values for the same entity. Funds may engage independent valuation firms for the calculation, but the volatility assumption is not negotiable. This prevents the taxable base from being inflated by investor protections that do not accrue to founders, employees, or common shareholders.

* Convertible Instrument Floor: For entities that have raised capital exclusively through unpriced instruments — including Simple Agreements for Future Equity (SAFEs), convertible notes, and other deferred-conversion securities — the taxable EV base is set at no less than the aggregate capital raised through such instruments, appreciated at a statutory annual rate of 10% compounded from the date of each instrument's issuance. The appreciation rate reflects the long-run expected return on venture capital and ensures the floor scales with time rather than remaining static. Entities whose current enterprise value has fallen below the appreciated floor — evidenced by two consecutive fiscal years of declining gross revenue — may petition for a reduced floor based on audited financials. Because the floor will understate the true enterprise value of high-growth firms, companies may establish a lower effective tax base by electing into the LP Disclosure Peg (via a priced round) or the QLE Safe Harbor at any time. The floor is deliberately crude: honest disclosure always produces a more accurate — and typically lower — result.

## **2.2 The QLE Safe Harbor**

Private companies can confirm their EV Growth Tax base by holding a Qualified Liquidity Event (QLE) at least once every two years through tender offers or secondary market transactions. Empirical pricing from an arm's-length transaction is accepted as the definitive taxable base, eliminating uncertainty and audit risk.

* QLE Back-Fill Rule: When a previously non-disclosing entity establishes its first taxable EV through a QLE — including an IPO filing, acquisition, or qualifying secondary transaction — the resulting valuation is not treated as single-year EV growth. Instead, the taxable appreciation is allocated across all prior fiscal years during which the entity operated without an LP-reported valuation or QLE, weighted by each year's gross revenue as a proportion of cumulative gross revenue over the non-disclosure period. Revenue data is available from corporate tax filings regardless of disclosure status, requiring no additional reporting burden. This revenue-weighted allocation tracks the entity's actual growth trajectory rather than assuming linear appreciation, producing a more accurate year-by-year liability than straight-line interpolation. The existing Deemed Interest Surcharge (Section 2.3) applies to the back-filled amounts. This eliminates the single-year dilution cliff that would otherwise create structural incentives to delay public listing or avoid liquidity events.

## **2.3 Non-Compliance Penalty**

Private entities above the $10M exemption threshold that fail to establish a taxable EV through any of the three reporting mechanisms (LP Disclosure Peg, QLE Safe Harbor, or Federal Valuation Office appraisal) are subject to the following enforcement structure:

* Upon eventual determination of EV — whether through IRS audit, voluntary late disclosure, or a qualifying transaction such as an IPO, acquisition, or secondary sale — the standard EV Growth Tax is assessed on all back years of non-compliance, plus a Non-Compliance Surcharge of 25% of the calculated tax liability for each year of non-reporting.

* A Deemed Interest Surcharge of 5% compounded annually is applied to all back taxes and non-compliance surcharges, calculated from the original due date of each year's filing.

* The QLE Back-Fill Rule (Section 2.2) applies to the allocation of back-year liability, using the entity's revenue-weighted growth trajectory to distribute the taxable appreciation across the non-compliance period.

* The combined effect of the 25% surcharge and 5% compounding interest makes non-reporting strictly more expensive than any of the three compliant reporting options in every scenario. The Federal Valuation Office provides appraisals at no cost to the entity; non-compliance is therefore a choice to incur penalties rather than accept a free service.

## **2.4 Small Business Exemption and Pass-Through Treatment**

Entities with an Enterprise Value at or below $10 Million are exempt from the EV Growth Tax. Above the $10M threshold, the full 20% rate applies to all EV growth. The exemption eliminates compliance burden for genuinely small businesses — the category least able to absorb administrative overhead and least likely to generate meaningful revenue for the AMCF. The scrip payment mechanism ensures that the 20% rate above the threshold imposes no cash burden on mid-market firms; combined with the free appraisal provided by the Federal Valuation Office (Section 2.5), the effective compliance cost for a $15M business crossing the threshold for the first time is zero.

* Pass-Through Entities: S-corporations, partnerships, limited liability companies, and other pass-through entities with an enterprise value exceeding $10 Million are treated as C-corporations for purposes of the EV Growth Tax. The entity — not its individual owners — is the taxable unit, and its EV growth is subject to the standard 20% rate paid in scrip. This prevents high-value enterprises from using pass-through status to escape the EV Growth Tax while retaining the pass-through exemption for small businesses below the $10M threshold, where the pass-through structure serves its intended purpose of simplifying tax compliance for genuinely small operations.

## **2.5 The Federal Valuation Office**

The Federal Valuation Office (FVO) is established as an independent statutory agency responsible for business enterprise valuations and land value assessments across the United States. The FVO serves two of the Accord's core revenue mechanisms: the EV Growth Tax (business valuations for private entities) and the Land Value Tax (unimproved land value assessments under Section 3.2). Modeled on the Australian Valuer-General system — where independent statutory officers in each state produce property valuations separate from the tax collection authority — the FVO is structurally independent from the IRS to ensure that valuations serve accuracy rather than revenue maximization.

* Institutional Independence: The FVO is chartered as an independent federal agency, not a division of the IRS or Treasury. The Federal Valuer-General is nominated by the President and confirmed by the Senate for a fixed 7-year term, removable only for cause (malfeasance, incapacity, or demonstrated systemic inaccuracy). This mirrors the independence protections afforded to the Federal Reserve Chair and insulates the office from political pressure to inflate valuations.

* Dual Mandate: The FVO conducts (a) enterprise valuations for private entities that elect the appraisal option under Section 1.1, and (b) unimproved land value assessments for all parcels subject to the Land Value Tax (Section 3.2). Combining both functions in a single agency creates economies of scale in valuation expertise, data infrastructure, and professional staffing — the same skill set that values commercial real estate also values the land beneath it.

* Default Appraisal Provider: For private entities above the $10M exemption threshold that lack LP-reported valuations or qualifying transactions, the FVO provides business valuations at no cost to the entity, funded by general appropriations. This eliminates the appraiser-shopping incentive that arises when companies hire and pay their own valuers. Entities that disagree with the FVO's valuation may commission their own Qualified Independent Appraisal from an accredited private valuation professional (ABV, ASA, or equivalent credential) and submit it as a challenge; the burden of proof rests with the entity, and the challenge threshold is a demonstrated divergence of more than 15% from the FVO's determination.

* Professional Standards: All FVO valuations are conducted in accordance with Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice (USPAP) and are subject to peer review by an independent panel of private-sector valuation professionals appointed by the Congressional Regulation Office. The FVO publishes annual accuracy reports comparing its valuations against subsequent arm's-length transaction prices, creating a public track record of institutional accuracy that Congress can oversee.

* Staffing and Scale: The UK's Valuation Office Agency valued 2.1 million commercial properties and 26.8 million residential properties with approximately 3,800 staff. The FVO's business valuation mandate — covering an estimated 200,000 to 400,000 private entities above the $10M threshold — is a fraction of this scale, though the land value assessment mandate will require comparable infrastructure. Initial staffing is estimated at 3,000 to 5,000 accredited valuation professionals, scaling with the LVT assessment rollout during Phase 1. The FVO is the largest single institutional investment required by the Accord and is budgeted within Phase 1 implementation costs.

* OPM Volatility Index: The FVO maintains and publishes the index of comparable public company equity volatilities used in the Common Stock Equivalent Requirement (Section 2.1), replacing the IRS Office of Research in this function. Centralizing the volatility index within the valuation agency rather than the tax collection agency ensures methodological consistency and independence.

## **2.6 International Framework — US Subsidiary Requirement**

Any entity that operates a trade or business in the United States or maintains a listing on a US-regulated securities exchange must do so through a US-incorporated subsidiary. That subsidiary is subject to the EV Growth Tax under the same mandatory reporting hierarchy as all other US entities (Section 1.1). Foreign-incorporated parent entities are not directly subject to the EV Growth Tax.

* Exchange Listing Condition: No foreign-incorporated entity may maintain a primary or secondary listing, or an American Depositary Receipt (ADR) program, on any US-regulated securities exchange (including NYSE, NASDAQ, and their successors) without establishing and maintaining a US-incorporated subsidiary registered with the SEC. The subsidiary must be the issuer of record for US-traded securities and must be subject to US securities law, US courts, and US shareholder protections. This closes the variable interest entity (VIE) and shell company structures that currently allow foreign entities to access US capital markets without enforceable US legal obligations to investors.

* Operational Presence: Any foreign entity that conducts business in the United States — including maintaining employees, offices, manufacturing facilities, warehouses, or customer-facing operations — must do so through a US-incorporated subsidiary. IRS Controlled Group Aggregation (Section 1.3) applies across all US subsidiaries sharing common foreign ownership, preventing fragmentation into sub-threshold entities.

* Subsidiary Valuation: The enterprise value of each US subsidiary is established through the standard mandatory reporting hierarchy — observed market data for publicly traded subsidiaries, LP Disclosure Peg or QLE Safe Harbor for subsidiaries with institutional investors, or Federal Valuation Office appraisal (Section 2.5) for all others. The FVO appraises US subsidiaries of foreign parents based on the actual operations, assets, revenue, and workforce of the US entity — not on the foreign parent's internal transfer pricing allocations or intercompany accounting. This neutralizes the primary mechanism by which foreign multinationals currently suppress US-sourced taxable income.

* Anti-Inversion Provision: US-incorporated entities that redomicile to a foreign jurisdiction but retain substantial US operations are treated as domestic entities for EV Growth Tax purposes if, following the redomiciliation, (a) 25% or more of the entity's workforce remains in the United States, or (b) 25% or more of the entity's tangible assets are located in the United States, or (c) 25% or more of the entity's gross revenue is sourced from US customers. This strengthens existing anti-inversion protections under Section 7874 of the Internal Revenue Code and ensures that redomiciliation without genuine economic relocation does not escape the EV Growth Tax.

*Jurisdictional framing: The EV Growth Tax is assessed on US-incorporated entities — including subsidiaries of foreign parents — as an excise on the privilege of operating under US corporate law. The tax does not reach foreign parent entities or their non-US operations. This is consistent with the long-standing principle that the United States taxes entities organized under its laws on a worldwide basis, while taxing foreign entities only on income (or, under the Accord, enterprise value growth) effectively connected with US operations conducted through a US legal presence.*

# **Part 3: Broad-Based Revenue Enhancements**

Three revenue streams supplement the EV Growth Tax and provide the primary funding base for the universal prebate and social infrastructure spending. Together they are estimated to generate approximately $2.0 to $2.3 trillion annually at full implementation maturity.

## **3.1 Value-Added Tax (VAT) — Flat 10%, Universal Base**

A 10% VAT applies to all goods and services without exemption. Regressivity is addressed through the elevated universal prebate rather than point-of-sale exemptions — a mathematically equivalent outcome without administrative friction.

* Annual revenue at full compliance: $1.65 to $1.75 trillion.

* Year 1 realistic yield at 75–80% compliance: $1.2 to $1.35 trillion, scaling to full rate by Year 3 to 4\.

* Implementation timeline: 12 to 15 months using modern cloud infrastructure and the existing Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement as collection backbone.

## **3.2 Land Value Tax (LVT) — 3% on Unimproved Value**

A 3% annual tax on the unimproved value of land. The first $250,000 of unimproved land value for an owner-occupied primary residence is exempt.

* Estimated taxable base: $12 to $15 trillion after homeowner exemption.

* Year 1 realistic yield: $150 to $200 billion, scaling to $360 to $450 billion annually by Year 5 to 6 as assessment infrastructure matures.

* The LVT penalizes speculative land banking and forces productive development, structurally lowering residential land costs over time — the most direct federal lever for housing affordability without explicit subsidy.

* Unimproved land values are assessed by the Federal Valuation Office (Section 2.5), which maintains the national land value assessment roll in addition to its business valuation mandate. The FVO's land assessment methodology is modeled on the Australian Valuer-General system, using mass appraisal techniques calibrated to comparable sales data and updated on a rolling 3-year cycle.

*Constitutional grounding: The LVT is defensible as an indirect excise on the privilege of holding exclusive sovereign-backed title to geographic space. This framing, supported by Hylton v. United States (1796) and the Founders' familiarity with English land rent taxation, places the LVT outside the apportionment requirement of the direct tax clause.*

## **3.3 Payroll Donut Hole Fix**

The 12.4% Social Security payroll tax is reapplied to all earned income above $400,000, closing the current cap that exempts high-income earners from contributing to the system. Estimated yield: $200 to $250 billion annually.

# **Part 5: The American Capital Fund (AMCF) — Governance and Structure**

## **5.1 Charter and Institutional Form**

The American Capital Fund (AMCF) is chartered as a government corporation modeled on the FDIC and TVA — institutions with operational independence, politically accountable leadership, and statutory mandates that constrain discretion without requiring full executive control.

* The AMCF is governed by a Board of Directors subject to Senate confirmation, ensuring political accountability without day-to-day political interference.

* Spending is governed by the statutory priority waterfall (see Part 7). Modifying waterfall priorities requires a supermajority vote of both chambers.

* The AMCF is a strictly passive investor. It holds equity and receives dividends. It exercises no voting rights on the corporations in which it holds stakes. Governance and worker voice are handled entirely through the codetermination architecture.

## **5.2 Investment Mandate**

The AMCF's equity holdings are governed by an indexed passive mandate. Scrip received from the EV Growth Tax is held as received and not actively traded. Dividend income is the primary cash generation mechanism. The AMCF does not engage in activist investing, proxy contests, or directional bets on individual companies. Its institutional analogue is the Norwegian Government Pension Fund Global, adapted to the American constitutional structure.

* International Diversification: The AMCF accumulates international exposure through two organic channels without selling US corporate equity. First, cash proceeds from Tier 2 Closely-Held Cash Elections (Section 1.4) may be deployed into international index funds rather than US equities, at the discretion of the AMCF Board subject to the passive mandate. Second, scrip received from US subsidiaries of foreign-parented companies (Section 2.6) — including entities like Toyota USA, Samsung Electronics America, Nestlé USA — inherently provides exposure to internationally managed enterprises operating on US soil. Combined, these channels are projected to produce 15 to 25% international portfolio exposure at maturity without requiring the politically sensitive step of selling American corporate equity to purchase foreign assets. This diversification provides countercyclical protection: global assets do not move in lockstep with US GDP, ensuring the AMCF retains value during domestic economic downturns when tax revenue also falls.

## **5.3 The American Commonwealth Index (ACI)**

The American Commonwealth Index is the AMCF's second performance dimension alongside financial return. The ACI measures national wellbeing through a composite of five weighted metrics and serves three distinct functions: it is the distribution trigger for citizen AMCF grants, the performance benchmark for government employee and congressional compensation, and the public dashboard that makes the connection between governance quality and personal financial outcomes visible to every American.

The ACI is calculated and published by an independent statistical body drawing exclusively on BEA, BLS, CDC, and Census data sources. The CRO's evaluation division conducts annual peer-reviewed audits of the index calculation methodology. No administrative discretion is exercised in index calculation; all inputs are publicly reported government statistics.

*Distribution trigger: Citizen AMCF grants are issued at full rate when the ACI shows year-over-year improvement. When the ACI is flat or declining, the distribution rate is reduced by 50% and the withheld portion is redirected to the Healthcare Reserve. This ensures citizen ownership income reflects genuine national progress rather than financial market performance alone.*

## **5.4 Citizen AMCF Grants — Universal Per Capita Ownership**

Every American citizen receives an annual AMCF unit grant, denominated in the fund's equity units. Grants are universal, equal per capita, and unconditional. Progressivity is achieved entirely through the tax system applied to vested returns, not through means-testing the grant itself.

* Grant size: 15% of AMCF annual distributable income, divided equally among all citizens, with a statutory minimum of $500 per capita annually regardless of fund size. During Phase 1, when distributable income is insufficient to fund the $500 minimum from the AMCF's own returns, the shortfall is funded through the SPV bridge (Section 1.4). The minimum floor ensures every American holds a visible and growing ownership stake from Year 1, building the political constituency for the Accord before the fund reaches self-sustaining scale. At Phase 2 AMCF scale ($175–250 billion distributable), the 15% formula naturally exceeds the $500 floor, producing approximately $200–375 annually per capita from distributable income alone, scaling toward $750–1,200 per capita at Phase 3 maturity.

* Adult accounts: Units vest on a 3-year staggered schedule (33% per year). Vested units may be held, rolled into qualified retirement accounts, or redeemed at AMCF net asset value.

* Custodial accounts: Units issued to minors are held in Treasury Direct custodial accounts until age 18, at which point the full accumulated portfolio transfers to the citizen's personal AMCF account. No parental access mechanism exists; the account belongs to the child.

* Universal lifecycle: A child born today accumulates 18 years of compounding AMCF grants before reaching adulthood. With the $500 per capita annual minimum and 6.5% compounding over 18 years, the custodial account reaches approximately $15,000 to $17,000 at transfer — replacing and improving upon the separate baby bond program, which is eliminated.

* ACI inflation damper: When CPI exceeds 3%, vesting continues on schedule but redemptions are paused. Vested units held during the pause earn a guaranteed 7% annual credit, payable in additional units. This converts the inflation brake from a perceived confiscation into a high-yield savings mechanism. Hardship distributions for medical emergencies, eviction prevention, and funeral expenses are exempt from the pause.

**⚠ Red Team Defense —** *Index gaming risk: With a composite ACI rather than a single metric, manipulation requires simultaneously gaming five independently measured government statistics from different agencies. The ACI weighting can be reviewed on a 5-year cycle by the CRO evaluation division with public comment, ensuring the measured components continue to track the underlying concept they represent.*

## **5.5 Healthcare Reserve**

A statutory directive requires that once AMCF distributable income exceeds Priority 1 obligations and the solvency brake threshold, a defined allocation of distributable surplus is directed to a dedicated Medicare Trust Fund Reserve before the Priority 3 discretionary unlock. The Healthcare Reserve serves as the AMCF's explicit long-term backstop for Medicare's actuarial gap.

* Target reserve: 10% of AMCF distributable income above Priority 1 obligations, activating in Phase 2\.

* Purpose: The reserve funds three specific healthcare infrastructure commitments: the National Rate Setting Commission capital, graduate medical education residency expansion (doubling GME slots), and the Strategic Capacity Retention program maintaining 15% ICU surge capacity at contractual retainer rates.

* Framing: The sovereign wealth fund built by American workers and taxpayers funds the healthcare security of American retirees. This closes the loop between the ownership economy and the social contract.

# **Part 9: The Care Economy Protection Fund — Healthcare Framework**

Federal healthcare spending is the fastest-growing component of the fiscal picture and the primary variable that can prevent fiscal crossover regardless of AMCF performance. A public option available to all Americans at reimbursement rates set at Medicare \+ 25%. The 25% premium above pure Medicare rates is calibrated to keep rural hospitals solvent while still undercutting private insurer reimbursement rates by 20 to 30%.

* Risk Equalization Board: Mandatory risk adjustment between the public option and private insurers. If a private insurer enrolls a healthy population, the Board transfers funds to the public option. ## **11.1 Debt Sustainability — The Survivable Buildup**

The Accord requires the national debt to grow before it shrinks. Gross debt rises from approximately $36 trillion at inception toward a peak of $65 to $68 trillion around Year 20 to 21, before the AMCF-driven surplus begins paying it down. This trajectory will draw scrutiny. The honest case for survivability rests on four structural arguments.

### **The Trajectory Matters More Than the Level**

Every major credit rating downgrade of the United States — S\&P in 2011, Fitch in 2023, Moody's in 2025 — cited the same fundamental concern: not the level of debt, but the absence of any credible plan to reverse its growth. Moody's stated that "successive administrations and Congress have failed to agree on measures to reverse the trend of large annual fiscal deficits and growing interest costs." The Accord is the first proposal that provides a self-executing reversal mechanism. The AMCF compounds equity automatically, generates growing dividend income without legislative action, and the statutory spending waterfall directs surplus to debt reduction through the solvency brake. Rating agencies evaluate trajectory and institutional credibility, not point-in-time debt levels. A sovereign that can demonstrate a mathematically grounded path to surplus is rated differently than one that depends on future political will that has repeatedly failed to materialize.

Under current law (CBO March 2025 baseline), federal debt reaches 107% of GDP by 2029, 120% by 2036, and 156% by 2055 — with no mechanism to ever stop growing. The Accord's debt-to-GDP ratio peaks at approximately 170% around Year 20 and then declines. The peak is higher than the current-law projection at that same point in time, but the current-law projection is still rising at that point while the Accord's is falling. The crossover in trajectory is the critical difference.

### **Interest Rate Reflexivity — The Doom Loop Defense**

Rising debt increases the interest rate the government pays on new borrowing. The Dallas Federal Reserve's most recent estimate (2025) finds that each percentage point increase in the debt-to-GDP ratio raises long-term interest rates by approximately 3 basis points. At the Accord's peak debt-to-GDP of approximately 170%, the increase from the current 100% baseline would add roughly 210 basis points to Treasury rates — potentially pushing the blended borrowing cost from 3.5% toward 5.5 to 6%.

This reflexivity is real and must be acknowledged. Higher rates increase interest costs, which widen the deficit, which increases debt, which raises rates further. The question is whether the AMCF's compounding revenue growth outpaces the interest rate spiral. The model projects that it does: AMCF dividend income grows exponentially (roughly 15 to 20% annually as the fund compounds), while interest costs grow linearly with debt (roughly 3 to 4% annually as debt accumulates). The exponential curve eventually dominates the linear one — this is the mathematical basis for the crossover.

Peak interest as a percentage of total federal revenue reaches approximately 29% around Year 14 to 15, then declines as AMCF dividends flood the revenue base. For comparison, under current law the CBO projects interest costs rising to 6.3% of GDP by 2054 with no reversal — a permanently worsening ratio. The Accord's 29% peak is painful but temporary; the current trajectory's rising burden is permanent.

### **Net Sovereign Position — The Balance Sheet View**

Gross debt is the wrong metric for evaluating the Accord's fiscal health during the buildup period. The correct metric is the net sovereign position: gross debt minus AMCF equity holdings. By Year 10, gross debt reaches approximately $56 trillion but the AMCF holds $9 trillion in equity — net position is $47 trillion, comparable to the current gross debt level. By Year 15, gross debt is approximately $64 trillion but the AMCF holds $27 trillion — net position is $37 trillion, actually lower than today's gross debt. By Year 20, the AMCF holds $64 trillion against $68 trillion in gross debt — net sovereign position approaches zero. The United States transitions from a net debtor to a net creditor within 20 to 25 years.

Bond markets that evaluate sovereign creditworthiness on a net basis — as they do for Norway, Singapore, and other sovereign wealth fund countries — would see a rapidly improving balance sheet even during the period when gross debt is still growing. This is the structural advantage of the Accord's approach: it doesn't just promise to reduce debt through future spending cuts, it simultaneously builds an asset portfolio that improves the sovereign balance sheet from Day 1.

### **The AMCF as Non-Tradeable Collateral**

The AMCF's equity holdings serve as implicit collateral for the national debt. Unlike a promise to cut spending or raise taxes — which depends on future political will — the AMCF's assets are real, liquid, and growing. A bondholder evaluating US creditworthiness can observe the AMCF's holdings in real time. If the United States ever faced a genuine debt servicing crisis (an extremely unlikely scenario for a sovereign that borrows in its own currency), the AMCF could liquidate portions of its portfolio to service debt. This option would never need to be exercised, but its existence fundamentally changes the risk calculus for bondholders.

The AMCF also functions as a structural market stabilizer. At maturity, it holds 15 to 20% of US corporate equity as a permanent, non-trading position. During market crashes, the AMCF's shares do not enter the market as selling pressure. This removes roughly one-fifth of potential forced-selling volume during drawdowns, reducing crash severity and shortening recovery time. The stabilization effect benefits the entire financial system, including the Treasury market, by reducing systemic risk and the probability of cascading financial crises that would impair sovereign creditworthiness.

### **Comparison to Current-Law Trajectory**

The relevant comparison is not "Accord versus an idealized balanced budget" but "Accord versus what actually happens under current law." The CBO projects that under current policy, deficits grow from $1.9 trillion in 2026 to $3.1 trillion by 2036, debt reaches 175% of GDP by the mid-2050s, and interest costs consume a permanently rising share of revenue — with no mechanism to reverse any of these trends. The Accord's temporary buildup period, while real, leads to a structural surplus and declining debt. The current trajectory leads to a permanent and accelerating debt spiral. Any honest evaluation of the Accord's fiscal risk must be measured against this baseline, not against a hypothetical balanced budget that the political system has demonstrated no ability to achieve.
