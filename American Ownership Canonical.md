# **Part 1: The Revenue Engine — The Grand Swap**

The core corporate taxation reform eliminates profit-based taxation in favor of a levy on enterprise value growth. This structural shift eliminates the primary mechanisms of tax avoidance — profit shifting, earnings management, and debt-financed suppression of taxable equity — while preserving full operating liquidity for productive reinvestment.

## **1.1 The EV Growth Tax**

Tax Rate: 20% on the increase in Enterprise Value (EV), calculated via a rolling 3-year high-water mark.

Enterprise Value is determined through mandatory reporting, with the method varying by entity type:

* Publicly traded entities: EV is calculated from observed market data — market capitalization of all outstanding equity securities plus total debt minus cash and cash equivalents — using the fiscal year-end closing price. The taxable base is the change in this observed EV, subject to the high-water mark. No additional reporting is required; the IRS computes EV from existing SEC filings.

* Private entities above the $10M exemption threshold: EV must be established annually through one of three mechanisms — (a) the LP Disclosure Peg (Section 2.1), using the highest investor-reported valuation on a common-stock-equivalent basis; (b) the QLE Safe Harbor (Section 2.2), using empirical pricing from an arm's-length transaction conducted at least once every two years; or (c) a valuation conducted by the Federal Valuation Office (Section 2.5), an independent statutory agency that provides business enterprise valuations at no cost to the entity. The FVO valuation is the default for any private business without investor reporting or a recent qualifying transaction; entities that disagree may challenge the FVO determination with their own Qualified Independent Appraisal at their own expense.

* Non-compliance: Entities that fail to report are subject to the Non-Compliance Penalty (Section 2.3). The penalty is assessed upon eventual determination of EV through IRS audit, voluntary disclosure, or a qualifying transaction, and is designed to make non-reporting strictly more expensive than any of the three reporting options above.

Taxing Enterprise Value rather than Market Capitalization provides capital structure neutrality. A firm cannot suppress its taxable base by over-leveraging with private credit, because debt is additive to EV rather than deductive. This closes the most aggressively exploited gap in current corporate taxation.

* Definition of Debt: For purposes of the EV formula, "debt" includes all interest-bearing financial obligations — corporate bonds, term loans, revolving credit facilities, interbank borrowings, repurchase agreements, commercial paper, and subordinated notes. It excludes customer deposits held by regulated depository institutions, policyholder reserves and claim liabilities held by regulated insurance companies, client funds held in fiduciary or custodial capacity by regulated broker-dealers, any other liability subject to federal or state prudential capital requirements, and structured promissory notes issued to satisfy Phantom Stock Unit (PSU) redemptions under the codetermination framework. The excluded liabilities share a common characteristic: they are either the core product or operating obligation of a regulated financial business, or a statutory obligation arising from the Accord's own worker equity architecture — not discretionary financing choices. This ensures a single EV formula applies uniformly to all entities — financial and non-financial — without sector-specific carve-outs, while preventing the formula from producing inflated enterprise values through liabilities that are regulatory or statutory in origin rather than capital-structure decisions.

## **1.2 Payment Mechanism — Corporate Scrip Escrow**

* Tax liability is satisfied through issuance of new corporate scrip into a 3-Year Sustained Accrual Escrow. The scrip represents a share of new value created (EV growth), not a transfer of existing ownership — existing shareholders retain their full claim on the company's pre-growth enterprise value. The AMCF receives equity proportional to 20% of the value increase, and existing shareholders retain 80% of all new value created plus 100% of pre-existing value.

* Shares vest to the American Capital Fund (AMCF) at 33% per year over three years.

* Scrip vests unconditionally once issued. If a company's valuation declines below the high-water mark during or after the vesting window, the AMCF absorbs the downside as an equity holder alongside all other shareholders. The high-water mark (Section 1.1) ensures no additional tax is assessed until the prior peak is exceeded, but previously issued scrip is not canceled. This mirrors standard tax finality — realized tax obligations are not refunded when subsequent conditions change — and eliminates the gaming incentive that a conditional vesting structure would create.

*Constitutional framing: The EV Growth Tax is structured as a federal excise on the privilege of operating as a juridical person under state and federal law, measured by the appreciation in that entity's enterprise value. This mirrors the corporate excise upheld in Flint v. Stone Tracy Co. (1911). For publicly traded entities, the taxable base is derived from observable, market-determined prices. For private entities, the taxable base is established through the entity's own reporting — investor disclosures, arm's-length transactions, or independent appraisals — ensuring that the government taxes a value the entity itself has affirmed. The scrip payment mechanism avoids realization problems by compelling a fractional ownership transfer rather than demanding cash on unrealized gains.*

### **The Social Infrastructure Return**

The EV Growth Tax is best understood not as a corporate tax but as a social infrastructure return. A company's enterprise value growth comes from multiple sources. Some of it is genuinely the company's own doing — better products, smarter management, operational efficiency. But a substantial portion comes from factors the company did not create: the American consumer market (330 million affluent consumers whose purchasing power is the product of decades of public investment), the legal infrastructure (contract enforcement, IP protection, bankruptcy law), the educated workforce (publicly funded K-12 and subsidized higher education), the transportation network, the financial system, the internet backbone, and the basic research funded by NIH, NSF, and DARPA that underlies most technology and pharmaceutical innovation.

This is the Georgist argument extended beyond land. The EV Growth Tax applies the same principle to corporate value: a portion of every company's growth is a return on the social ecosystem in which it operates, not purely a return on the company's own effort. The 79/21 split reflects an estimate that roughly 21% of value creation in the American economy is a return on social infrastructure rather than private effort. The company keeps 79% as a reward for its own innovation, risk-taking, and execution. The public captures 21% as a return on the market ecosystem that made the growth possible.

The AMCF converts this return into permanent public co-ownership. The AMCF establishes co-ownership — the public has a legitimate property claim on the value its own infrastructure generates.

### **Competitive Dynamics — No Asymmetry Between New and Existing Firms**

A natural question arises: does the Growth Tax disadvantage startups relative to incumbents? Once an incumbent has reached the 21% AMCF ownership equilibrium, the Growth Tax ceases for that company. A startup founded after Year 19 faces a Growth Tax that the incumbent does not. This appears to create a competitive asymmetry.

The asymmetry is illusory. The economic outcome for private shareholders is identical in both cases. An incumbent whose Growth Tax has ceased still has the AMCF as a 21% owner. When the incumbent grows by $100 million, the AMCF's 21% stake appreciates by $21 million and private shareholders' 79% stake appreciates by $79 million. A startup subject to the Growth Tax grows by $100 million, issues $21 million in scrip to the AMCF, and private shareholders retain $79 million. In both cases, private shareholders capture exactly 79% of every dollar of value created. The mechanism differs — appreciation of existing holdings versus new scrip issuance — but the economic result is identical.

An investor evaluating the incumbent versus the startup sees the same 79% capture rate on marginal growth in both cases. There is no rational basis for preferring the incumbent on Growth Tax grounds, because the AMCF's co-ownership reduces private value capture by exactly the same 21% whether the ownership was established through historical scrip issuance or through appreciation of an existing stake.

The Accord additionally advantages startups through two structural features. First, entities below $10 million in enterprise value are completely exempt from the Growth Tax — the most fragile phase of a company's life is entirely shielded. Second, the elimination of the corporate income tax removes the sophisticated tax engineering (depreciation gaming, transfer pricing, offshore profit shifting, interest deduction stacking) that currently allows large incumbents to achieve effective CIT rates far below the statutory 21% while startups with domestic-only operations and no tax lawyers pay rates closer to the full statutory rate. Under the Accord, the competitive tax playing field is genuinely level for the first time.

### **Existing Shareholders — The Dilution That Makes You Richer**

A common objection: "The Growth Tax dilutes my 401(k). Every share in my retirement account is worth less because new shares are issued to the AMCF." This is mechanically true and economically misleading.

The dilution is real. Over the accumulation period, the AMCF's 21% stake is built through scrip issuance that dilutes all existing shareholders — including every American with a 401(k), IRA, or brokerage account. A portfolio worth $100,000 before the Accord is diluted to approximately $79,000 in ownership terms as the AMCF's stake reaches equilibrium.

But every American simultaneously receives AMCF equity units — distributed equally per capita regardless of existing wealth. Over 30 years, these units accumulate to hundreds of thousands of dollars per person. The question is whether the AMCF grants exceed the dilution loss.

For the median stock-owning household (portfolio value approximately $50,000): the 21% dilution reduces their portfolio by approximately $10,500 over the accumulation period. Their cumulative AMCF unit grants over the same period — growing from $500 per person at Year 1 to $25,924 per person at Year 30 — are worth several hundred thousand dollars per household member. The AMCF grants exceed the dilution by a factor of 20 to 50.

For the bottom 50% of households, who currently own essentially no stock at all, there is nothing to dilute. The AMCF provides pure gain — ownership where none existed before. A household with $0 in stock market wealth loses $0 to dilution and gains the full value of their AMCF unit grants.

For the top 10% of households (average portfolio approximately $3-5 million): the 21% dilution costs approximately $630,000-$1,050,000. Their AMCF unit grants are the same as everyone else's — perhaps $300,000-500,000 cumulative over 30 years. The dilution exceeds the AMCF grant. But these households also benefit from the 0% CIT (which increases corporate earnings and thus the value of their remaining 79% stake), PSU dividends if they're employed, and the general economic growth acceleration that the Accord produces. The net effect for the top 10% is positive by Year 5 (+$1.2 million in net worth by Year 30) despite the dilution.

The only households for whom dilution is a permanent net loss are the top 1% — whose portfolios are large enough that the 21% dilution overwhelms all offsetting benefits. Even for them, the loss is relative, not absolute: their wealth still grows from $16.7 million to $105.5 million over 30 years.

The headline: approximately 90% of American stockholders receive AMCF grants worth more than the dilution of their existing holdings. For the bottom 50%, the Accord creates ownership from nothing. For the top 40%, it replaces concentrated ownership with diversified ownership that includes both their (diluted) private portfolio and their (growing) AMCF account. Only the top 1% experiences a net reduction in total equity wealth — and even that reduction is from "extremely wealthy" to "extremely wealthy, slightly less so."

**⚠ Red Team Defense —** *"You're stealing from my 401(k)" objection: The Growth Tax does not touch any existing share. No shares are confiscated, transferred, or revalued. New shares are issued to the AMCF — the same mechanism companies use when they grant stock options, issue secondary offerings, or acquire other companies with stock. Dilution through new share issuance is a standard corporate finance event that happens thousands of times per year. The only difference is the recipient: instead of shares going to executives (stock options) or investment banks (secondary offerings), they go to the American people. Every American who holds a 401(k) is simultaneously receiving AMCF units that, for 90%+ of households, are worth more than the dilution. The net effect is positive wealth creation for the vast majority of stockholders.*

## **1.3 The Grand Swap**

* As the AMCF's organic dividend yield reaches self-sustaining levels by Year 6 to 8, the SPV unwinds. Treasury redeems its bond tranche using AMCF distributions, and the government retains the equity.

# **Part 2: The Private Market Protocol**

Non-traded entities are brought into the EV Growth Tax base through mandatory valuation reporting. Every private entity above the $10M exemption threshold must establish its taxable Enterprise Value annually through investor disclosures, arm's-length transactions, or independent appraisals. The protocol is designed so that compliance is simple and inexpensive relative to the penalties for non-reporting.

## **2.1 The LP Disclosure Peg**

For any private firm, the taxable EV base is strictly pegged to the highest Net Asset Value (NAV) or Fair Market Value (FMV) reported to any Limited Partner, creditor, or investor during the fiscal year. This eliminates the need for a separate government valuation apparatus.

* Common Stock Equivalent Requirement: All LP-reported valuations must be stated on a common-stock-equivalent basis, stripping out the economic value of liquidation preferences, participation rights, anti-dilution ratchets, and other senior security protections. The required methodology is the Option Pricing Model (OPM) backsolve, using equity volatility assumptions derived from a published index of comparable public company volatilities maintained by the Federal Valuation Office (Section 2.5). By standardizing the volatility input, the OPM output becomes replicable across valuation firms — eliminating the judgment divergence that would otherwise produce materially different common-equivalent values for the same entity. Funds may engage independent valuation firms for the calculation, but the volatility assumption is not negotiable. This prevents the taxable base from being inflated by investor protections that do not accrue to founders, employees, or common shareholders.

* Convertible Instrument Floor: For entities that have raised capital exclusively through unpriced instruments — including Simple Agreements for Future Equity (SAFEs), convertible notes, and other deferred-conversion securities — the taxable EV base is set at no less than the aggregate capital raised through such instruments, appreciated at a statutory annual rate of 10% compounded from the date of each instrument's issuance. The appreciation rate reflects the long-run expected return on venture capital and ensures the floor scales with time rather than remaining static. Entities whose current enterprise value has fallen below the appreciated floor — evidenced by two consecutive fiscal years of declining gross revenue — may petition for a reduced floor based on audited financials. Because the floor will understate the true enterprise value of high-growth firms, companies may establish a lower effective tax base by electing into the LP Disclosure Peg (via a priced round) or the QLE Safe Harbor at any time. The floor is deliberately crude: honest disclosure always produces a more accurate — and typically lower — result.

## **2.2 The QLE Safe Harbor**

Private companies can confirm their EV Growth Tax base by holding a Qualified Liquidity Event (QLE) at least once every two years through tender offers or secondary market transactions. Empirical pricing from an arm's-length transaction is accepted as the definitive taxable base, eliminating uncertainty and audit risk.

* QLE Back-Fill Rule: When a previously non-disclosing entity establishes its first taxable EV through a QLE — including an IPO filing, acquisition, or qualifying secondary transaction — the resulting valuation is not treated as single-year EV growth. Instead, the taxable appreciation is allocated across all prior fiscal years during which the entity operated without an LP-reported valuation or QLE, weighted by each year's gross revenue as a proportion of cumulative gross revenue over the non-disclosure period. Revenue data is available from corporate tax filings regardless of disclosure status, requiring no additional reporting burden. This revenue-weighted allocation tracks the entity's actual growth trajectory rather than assuming linear appreciation, producing a more accurate year-by-year liability than straight-line interpolation. The existing Deemed Interest Surcharge (Section 2.3) applies to the back-filled amounts. This eliminates the single-year dilution cliff that would otherwise create structural incentives to delay public listing or avoid liquidity events.

## **2.3 Non-Compliance Penalty**

Private entities above the $10M exemption threshold that fail to establish a taxable EV through any of the three reporting mechanisms (LP Disclosure Peg, QLE Safe Harbor, or Federal Valuation Office appraisal) are subject to the following enforcement structure:

* Upon eventual determination of EV — whether through IRS audit, voluntary late disclosure, or a qualifying transaction such as an IPO, acquisition, or secondary sale — the standard EV Growth Tax is assessed on all back years of non-compliance, plus a Non-Compliance Surcharge of 25% of the calculated tax liability for each year of non-reporting.

* A Deemed Interest Surcharge of 5% compounded annually is applied to all back taxes and non-compliance surcharges, calculated from the original due date of each year's filing.

* The QLE Back-Fill Rule (Section 2.2) applies to the allocation of back-year liability, using the entity's revenue-weighted growth trajectory to distribute the taxable appreciation across the non-compliance period.

* The combined effect of the 25% surcharge and 5% compounding interest makes non-reporting strictly more expensive than any of the three compliant reporting options in every scenario. The Federal Valuation Office provides appraisals at no cost to the entity; non-compliance is therefore a choice to incur penalties rather than accept a free service.

## **2.4 Small Business Exemption and Pass-Through Treatment**

Entities with an Enterprise Value at or below $10 Million are exempt from the EV Growth Tax. Above the $10M threshold, the full 21% rate applies to all EV growth. The exemption eliminates compliance burden for genuinely small businesses — the category least able to absorb administrative overhead and least likely to generate meaningful revenue for the AMCF. The scrip payment mechanism ensures that the 21% rate above the threshold imposes no cash burden on mid-market firms; combined with the free appraisal provided by the Federal Valuation Office (Section 2.5), the effective compliance cost for a $15M business crossing the threshold for the first time is zero.

* Pass-Through Entities: S-corporations, partnerships, limited liability companies, and other pass-through entities with an enterprise value exceeding $10 Million are treated as C-corporations for purposes of the EV Growth Tax. The entity — not its individual owners — is the taxable unit, and its EV growth is subject to the standard 21% rate paid in scrip. This prevents high-value enterprises from using pass-through status to escape the EV Growth Tax while retaining the pass-through exemption for small businesses below the $10M threshold, where the pass-through structure serves its intended purpose of simplifying tax compliance for genuinely small operations.

## **1.9 Competitive Dynamics — Startups, Incumbents, and the 80/20 Equilibrium**

A concern arises regarding competitive dynamics between new and existing firms. A startup founded after the AMCF reaches its 21% ownership cap faces a Growth Tax that an incumbent — whose AMCF stake is already at 20% — does not. Does this create an anti-startup bias?

The answer is no, because the economic outcome for private shareholders is identical regardless of whether a company has reached its ownership equilibrium.

Consider an incumbent where the AMCF already owns 21%. The company grows by $100 million. No scrip is issued, but the AMCF's 21% stake appreciates by $21 million. Private shareholders' 79% stake appreciates by $79 million. Private shareholders capture 79% of value creation.

Now consider a startup still in its Growth Tax ramp. The company grows by $100 million. Scrip worth $20 million is issued to the AMCF. Private shareholders retain $80 million in growth. Private shareholders capture 79% of value creation.

The mechanism differs — appreciation versus scrip issuance — but the cash flow to private shareholders is identical. An investor evaluating the incumbent versus the startup sees the same 79% capture rate on marginal growth in both cases. The Growth Tax during the ramp phase is not a penalty that incumbents avoid; it is the mechanical process by which the startup reaches the same ownership equilibrium that the incumbent has already reached. The 80/20 value split is universal — it applies to every company at all times, whether through the Growth Tax or through the AMCF's existing ownership stake.

This means the Growth Tax creates no competitive asymmetry between new and existing firms. The marginal investment incentive is identical whether a company is paying the Growth Tax or has already reached its 21% AMCF ownership equilibrium. The concern about startup disadvantage dissolves completely once the economics are traced through.

Additional structural advantages for startups under the Accord reinforce this neutrality. The $10 million exemption shields early-stage companies entirely. The 0% corporate income tax eliminates the sophisticated tax engineering (transfer pricing, offshore structures, depreciation gaming) that currently gives large incumbents effective tax rates far below what startups pay. And the Growth Tax's scrip payment mechanism imposes no cash burden on growth-stage companies — unlike the CIT, which drains operating cash from the moment a company becomes profitable. The Accord replaces a permanent, cash-draining, optimization-rewarding tax (CIT) with a temporary, cashless, unoptimizable one (Growth Tax) that converges to permanent zero-distortion co-ownership. That is inherently pro-startup.

## **2.5 The Federal Valuation Office**

The Federal Valuation Office (FVO) is established as an independent statutory agency responsible for business enterprise valuations and land value assessments across the United States. The FVO serves two of the Accord's core revenue mechanisms: the EV Growth Tax (business valuations for private entities) and the Land Value Tax (unimproved land value assessments under Section 3.2). Modeled on the Australian Valuer-General system — where independent statutory officers in each state produce property valuations separate from the tax collection authority — the FVO is structurally independent from the IRS to ensure that valuations serve accuracy rather than revenue maximization.

* Institutional Independence: The FVO is chartered as an independent federal agency, not a division of the IRS or Treasury. The Federal Valuer-General is nominated by the President and confirmed by the Senate for a fixed 7-year term, removable only for cause (malfeasance, incapacity, or demonstrated systemic inaccuracy). This mirrors the independence protections afforded to the Federal Reserve Chair and insulates the office from political pressure to inflate valuations.

* Dual Mandate: The FVO conducts (a) enterprise valuations for private entities that elect the appraisal option under Section 1.1, and (b) unimproved land value assessments for all parcels subject to the Land Value Tax (Section 3.2). Combining both functions in a single agency creates economies of scale in valuation expertise, data infrastructure, and professional staffing — the same skill set that values commercial real estate also values the land beneath it.

* Default Appraisal Provider: For private entities above the $10M exemption threshold that lack LP-reported valuations or qualifying transactions, the FVO provides business valuations at no cost to the entity, funded by general appropriations. This eliminates the appraiser-shopping incentive that arises when companies hire and pay their own valuers. Entities that disagree with the FVO's valuation may commission their own Qualified Independent Appraisal from an accredited private valuation professional (ABV, ASA, or equivalent credential) and submit it as a challenge; the burden of proof rests with the entity, and the challenge threshold is a demonstrated divergence of more than 15% from the FVO's determination.

* Professional Standards: All FVO valuations are conducted in accordance with Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice (USPAP) and are subject to peer review by an independent panel of private-sector valuation professionals appointed by the Congressional Regulation Office. The FVO publishes annual accuracy reports comparing its valuations against subsequent arm's-length transaction prices, creating a public track record of institutional accuracy that Congress can oversee.

* Staffing and Scale: The UK's Valuation Office Agency valued 2.1 million commercial properties and 26.8 million residential properties with approximately 3,800 staff. The FVO's business valuation mandate — covering an estimated 200,000 to 400,000 private entities above the $10M threshold — is a fraction of this scale, though the land value assessment mandate will require comparable infrastructure. Initial staffing is estimated at 3,000 to 5,000 accredited valuation professionals, scaling with the LVT assessment rollout during Phase 1. The FVO is the largest single institutional investment required by the Accord and is budgeted within Phase 1 implementation costs.

* OPM Volatility Index: The FVO maintains and publishes the index of comparable public company equity volatilities used in the Common Stock Equivalent Requirement (Section 2.1), replacing the IRS Office of Research in this function. Centralizing the volatility index within the valuation agency rather than the tax collection agency ensures methodological consistency and independence.

### **Formulary Apportionment for Foreign-Parented US Subsidiaries**

For any US subsidiary of a foreign parent entity, the FVO does not use the subsidiary's standalone financial statements, GAAP-reported assets, or internal transfer pricing to determine enterprise value. Instead, the FVO calculates the US subsidiary's appraised enterprise value through formulary apportionment:

`US Subsidiary Appraised EV = Global Parent EV × Apportionment Factor`

The Apportionment Factor is a weighted average of three indicators of real US economic activity:

| Factor | Weight | Calculation |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue | 50% | US-sourced revenue ÷ global revenue |
| Employment | 25% | US FTE headcount ÷ global FTE headcount |
| Assets | 25% | US physical assets (property, equipment, data centers) ÷ global physical assets |

Intercompany licensing fees, royalty payments, management charges, cost-sharing arrangements, and any other intra-group transfers are disregarded for apportionment purposes. Intellectual property location is explicitly excluded as a factor. The apportionment formula captures where the company sells (revenue), where it employs (workers), and where it builds (assets) — the three dimensions of real economic activity — while ignoring where it parks legal ownership of IP, which is the variable being gamed under current transfer pricing regimes.

This approach has established precedent. Several US states (California, Massachusetts, Illinois) already use formulary apportionment for state corporate income tax. The OECD's Pillar One framework moves in the same direction by proposing to allocate taxing rights based on where revenue is generated. The Accord applies this logic to enterprise value appraisal rather than profit allocation — a cleaner application because enterprise value is market-determined and harder to manipulate than reported profit.

Example: TechGlobal, incorporated in Ireland, is worth $500 billion. Its US subsidiary TechUS generates 60% of global revenue, employs 45% of the global workforce, and holds 40% of global physical assets. The apportionment factor is: (0.60 × 50%) + (0.45 × 25%) + (0.40 × 25%) = 0.30 + 0.1125 + 0.10 = 0.5125. TechUS is appraised at $256.25 billion — regardless of the fact that TechUS "owns" no IP, pays $20 billion annually in licensing fees to TechGlobal, and reports a GAAP net income of only $2 billion. The FVO's appraisal ignores the internal structure entirely and captures the economic value that US operations actually generate.

The Growth Tax applies to growth in the appraised value. The Equity Excise applies to the appraised value for worker equity purposes. The one-share-one-vote standard applies to TechUS's internal governance. Workers at TechUS hold a 26.6% effective voting stake sufficient to block any special resolution. The foreign parent votes its 58% stake in TechUS but cannot restructure, merge, dilute, or sell TechUS without worker consent.

**⚠ Red Team Defense —** *"The parent will hollow out US operations to reduce the apportionment factor" objection: Every employee moved abroad and every asset relocated reduces the apportionment factor — but also reduces the US subsidiary's capacity to generate US revenue, which is the primary factor at 50% weight. A company generating $30 billion from US customers cannot meaningfully reduce its revenue factor by moving engineers to India — the revenue comes from American consumers, not from where the code is written. The revenue anchor keeps the apportionment ratio stable even as operational footprints shift. And for the employment and asset factors: the NHC workforce development integration, the Accord's universal prebate reducing the cost of US labor, and the 0% CIT making the US the most attractive operating environment in the world all create economic gravity that pulls operations toward the US rather than away from it.*

## **2.6 International Framework — US Subsidiary Requirement**

Any entity that operates a trade or business in the United States or maintains a listing on a US-regulated securities exchange must do so through a US-incorporated subsidiary. That subsidiary is subject to the EV Growth Tax under the same mandatory reporting hierarchy as all other US entities (Section 1.1). Foreign-incorporated parent entities are not directly subject to the EV Growth Tax.

* Exchange Listing Condition: No foreign-incorporated entity may maintain a primary or secondary listing, or an American Depositary Receipt (ADR) program, on any US-regulated securities exchange (including NYSE, NASDAQ, and their successors) without establishing and maintaining a US-incorporated subsidiary registered with the SEC. The subsidiary must be the issuer of record for US-traded securities and must be subject to US securities law, US courts, and US shareholder protections. This closes the variable interest entity (VIE) and shell company structures that currently allow foreign entities to access US capital markets without enforceable US legal obligations to investors.

* Operational Presence: Any foreign entity that conducts business in the United States — including maintaining employees, offices, manufacturing facilities, warehouses, or customer-facing operations — must do so through a US-incorporated subsidiary. IRS Controlled Group Aggregation (Section 1.3) applies across all US subsidiaries sharing common foreign ownership, preventing fragmentation into sub-threshold entities.

* Subsidiary Valuation: The enterprise value of each US subsidiary is established through the standard mandatory reporting hierarchy — observed market data for publicly traded subsidiaries, LP Disclosure Peg or QLE Safe Harbor for subsidiaries with institutional investors, or Federal Valuation Office appraisal (Section 2.5) for all others. For foreign-parented US subsidiaries, the FVO applies formulary apportionment (Section 2.5) using a weighted multi-factor formula (50% US revenue share, 25% US employment share, 25% US asset share) to capture the true economic value of US operations regardless of internal transfer pricing, IP location, or intercompany fee structures. This neutralizes the primary mechanism by which foreign multinationals currently suppress US-sourced taxable income and prevents IP stripping from depressing the subsidiary's appraised value for Growth Tax and Equity Excise purposes.

* Anti-Inversion Provision: US-incorporated entities that redomicile to a foreign jurisdiction but retain substantial US operations are treated as domestic entities for EV Growth Tax purposes if, following the redomiciliation, (a) 25% or more of the entity's workforce remains in the United States, or (b) 25% or more of the entity's tangible assets are located in the United States, or (c) 25% or more of the entity's gross revenue is sourced from US customers. This strengthens existing anti-inversion protections under Section 7874 of the Internal Revenue Code and ensures that redomiciliation without genuine economic relocation does not escape the EV Growth Tax.

*Jurisdictional framing: The EV Growth Tax is assessed on US-incorporated entities — including subsidiaries of foreign parents — as an excise on the privilege of operating under US corporate law. The tax does not reach foreign parent entities or their non-US operations. This is consistent with the long-standing principle that the United States taxes entities organized under its laws on a worldwide basis, while taxing foreign entities only on income (or, under the Accord, enterprise value growth) effectively connected with US operations conducted through a US legal presence.*

# **Part 3: Broad-Based Revenue Enhancements**

Three revenue streams supplement the EV Growth Tax and provide the primary funding base for the universal prebate and social infrastructure spending. Together they are estimated to generate approximately $2.0 to $2.3 trillion annually at full implementation maturity.

## **3.1 Value-Added Tax (VAT) — 4%, Universal Base**

A 4% VAT applies to all goods and services without exemption. Regressivity is addressed through the elevated universal prebate rather than point-of-sale exemptions — a mathematically equivalent outcome without administrative friction.

The VAT rate is deliberately minimized. The Accord's rent tax portfolio (Sections 3.2 through 3.10) captures over $1.29 trillion annually from economically non-distortionary sources — land value, carbon emissions, financial transactions, spectrum usage, and resource extraction. These taxes have zero or near-zero deadweight loss because they target fixed-supply resources and rent-seeking behavior. The 4% VAT fills the remaining revenue gap while preserving the VAT's structural advantages: self-enforcing invoice chain, WTO-compliant border adjustment (exports zero-rated, imports taxed), and revenue that grows automatically with the economy.

At 4%, the $5,000 universal prebate fully offsets all VAT on $125,000 of annual consumption — covering approximately 80% of American households with zero net consumption tax burden. The visible consumer price impact is 4% rather than the 10% rate common in other proposals, dramatically reducing the risk of Federal Reserve overreaction to the one-time price level adjustment, consumer spending dampening, and small business cash flow compression that accompany higher VAT rates.

* Annual revenue at full compliance: $1.65 to $1.75 trillion.

* Year 1 realistic yield at 75–80% compliance: $1.2 to $1.35 trillion, scaling to full rate by Year 3 to 4\.

* Implementation timeline: 12 to 15 months using modern cloud infrastructure and the existing Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement as collection backbone.

## **3.2 Land Value Tax (LVT) — 10% on Unimproved Value**

A 10% annual tax on the unimproved value of land. The first $500,000 of unimproved land value for an owner-occupied primary residence is exempt.

The LVT is the Accord's primary non-VAT revenue engine, generating approximately $570 billion in Year 1. Economic theory identifies the LVT as the most efficient tax in existence — it has literally zero deadweight loss because the supply of land is fixed regardless of the tax rate. A higher rate cannot reduce the supply of land, discourage productive activity, or be passed through to tenants. The tax falls entirely on landowners and incentivizes productive land use by penalizing vacant lots, land banking, and speculative holding.

The $500,000 homeowner exemption shields the vast majority of American homeowners from any LVT burden. Median unimproved residential land value is approximately $80,000-120,000 nationally; even in high-cost metros, most primary residences have unimproved land values below $500,000. The exemption ensures the LVT falls primarily on commercial real estate, agricultural land held by large operations, vacant speculative holdings, and luxury residential properties — while making housing more affordable for everyone else by reducing speculative demand for land.

At 10%, land values will capitalize the tax downward — market land prices will decline as buyers factor in the ongoing tax obligation. This is the intended effect. Lower land prices make housing, commercial development, and agricultural entry more affordable. The Federal Valuation Office (Section 4.1) provides free, professional land assessments to ensure accuracy and prevent assessment-driven inequities.

## **3.3 Carbon Tax — $100 per Ton CO2**

A $100 per ton tax on carbon dioxide emissions from all sources. The atmospheric capacity to absorb CO2 is a fixed natural resource currently used for free; the carbon tax prices this commons. Estimated Year 1 revenue: approximately $348 billion, declining over time as the economy decarbonizes — by design.

The carbon tax is self-liquidating: as emissions fall in response to the price signal, revenue declines. This is a feature, not a bug. The Accord's fiscal architecture does not depend on permanent carbon revenue — it serves as a 15-20 year fiscal accelerator that pulls the crossover timeline forward while simultaneously driving the energy transition. By Year 15, carbon revenue will have declined to approximately $244 billion; by Year 25, approximately $190 billion.

The $5,000 universal prebate offsets the carbon tax's regressivity. Low-income households consume less carbon than average but receive the same per-capita prebate, ensuring they are net beneficiaries of the carbon pricing system.

## **3.4 Financial Transaction Tax (FTT) — 0.5% on Equity Transactions**

A 0.5% tax on all equity transactions, at parity with the United Kingdom's stamp duty on share purchases, which has operated since 1986 without measurable harm to London's status as a premier global financial center. Estimated Year 1 revenue: approximately $225 billion.

The FTT specifically targets high-frequency and speculative trading, which extracts rents from price discovery rather than adding productive value. The AMCF's 21% non-trading equity position makes the FTT structurally more viable in the United States than in any other country — the AMCF's shares never generate transaction costs because they never trade, reducing the liquidity impact of the tax on productive market activity.

Trading volume is expected to decline approximately 50% at the 0.5% rate, but the doubled rate more than compensates, producing net revenue of $225 billion — $117 billion more than a 0.1% rate would generate after behavioral adjustment.

## **3.5 Financial Stability Levy — 25 Basis Points on Systemically Important Institutions**

A 25 basis point annual levy on total assets of Global Systemically Important Banks (G-SIBs) operating in the United States. Estimated Year 1 revenue: approximately $48 billion.

The levy captures a portion of the implicit government subsidy extended to too-big-to-fail institutions. These banks borrow at artificially low rates because counterparties assume the government will not let them fail. This implicit guarantee — estimated at tens to hundreds of billions annually — is a pure economic rent from the government's credibility. The levy prices what is currently an unpriced public guarantee while incentivizing de-risking and reduced systemic interconnectedness.

## **3.6 Resource Royalty Reform — Market-Rate Extraction Fees**

Federal royalty rates on oil, gas, and mineral extraction from public lands are increased from the current 12.5% (unchanged since 1920) to 20.5%, reflecting market-rate compensation for the extraction of publicly owned natural resources. Estimated Year 1 revenue: approximately $48 billion.

## **3.7 Electromagnetic Spectrum Fees — 2% Annual Holding Fee**

A 2% annual fee on the assessed value of all licensed electromagnetic spectrum holdings. Estimated Year 1 revenue: approximately $15 billion. The radio spectrum is a natural resource with fixed supply; annual fees capture the ongoing rent value of exclusive use of a public commons, incentivize efficient spectrum utilization, and discourage hoarding.

## **3.8 Groundwater Extraction Fee — $25 per Acre-Foot**

A $25 per acre-foot fee on groundwater extraction from major aquifers. Estimated Year 1 revenue: approximately $2.25 billion. The primary purpose is allocative efficiency rather than revenue — pricing groundwater scarcity prevents catastrophic aquifer depletion (particularly the Ogallala Aquifer) and incentivizes conservation and reallocation from low-value to high-value uses.

## **3.9 Non-Carbon Pollution Fees**

Annual fees on nitrogen runoff, phosphorus discharge, and industrial pollutant emissions, capturing the value of environmental commons currently used for free. Estimated Year 1 revenue: approximately $20 billion.

## **3.10 Congestion Pricing — Federal Framework**

A federal framework encouraging adoption of congestion pricing in dense urban areas, with federal revenue sharing from participating jurisdictions. Estimated Year 1 revenue: approximately $12 billion. Road space during peak hours is a scarce public resource; pricing it reduces congestion, improves air quality, and funds transit alternatives.

# **Part 4: Individual Tax Base**

## **4.1 Capital Gains — Progressive Structure**

| Capital Gain Range | Rate |
| :---- | :---- |
| $0 – $200,000 | 0% |
| $200,000 – $1 Million | 20% |
| Above $1 Million | Ordinary income rates |

Rollover Provision: A 0% rate applies to any gain where proceeds are immediately reinvested into a qualified business. This preserves the incentive for productive capital redeployment while taxing extraction.

## **4.2 The Pledged Asset Tax**

Pledging appreciated assets as collateral for personal loans exceeding $5 million triggers a Constructive Sale. The loan amount is taxed immediately at the applicable realization rate, and the asset's cost basis is adjusted upward. This closes the Buy-Borrow-Die avoidance structure that allows accumulated wealth to compound indefinitely without tax realization.

## **4.3 Marginal Rate Adjustments**

The current standard deduction ($30,000 joint / $15,000 single) is fully retained. The prebate is not taxable income. AMCF unit grants are tax-free when received; appreciation is taxed as ordinary income upon liquidation under the two-rate system (25% below $1M, 50% above).

## **4.4 Mark-to-Market at Death — Eliminating the Stepped-Up Basis**

Death is treated as a realization event for all appreciated assets. The step-up in basis that currently allows heirs to inherit assets at fair market value — permanently erasing all unrealized capital gains — is eliminated. When an individual dies, their estate owes capital gains tax on all unrealized appreciation at the applicable rates (0% up to $200K, 20% up to $1M, ordinary rates above $1M), with a $5 million per-individual exclusion to protect family farms and small businesses from forced liquidation.

This closes the single largest remaining loophole in the capital gains system. Without it, the Accord's progressive capital gains structure (Section 4.1) can be partially avoided by simply never selling — holding appreciated assets through death and passing them tax-free to heirs. The Pledged Asset Tax (Section 4.2) closes the borrowing-against-assets channel; mark-to-market at death closes the intergenerational transfer channel. Together, they ensure that all appreciation in excess of $5 million is taxed within one lifetime regardless of realization strategy.

Multiple countries operate mark-to-market at death systems, including Canada (deemed disposition at death) and Australia (pre-1985 assets excepted). Estimated annual revenue: $40 to $60 billion.

## **4.5 Dynasty Trust and GRAT Reform**

Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts (GRATs) are reformed to require a minimum 10-year term and a minimum 25% remainder value, eliminating the "zeroed-out GRAT" structure that currently allows wealth transfers with no gift tax. Dynasty trusts are capped at 90 years (matching the common-law Rule Against Perpetuities), ending the use of perpetual trusts in states that have abolished the rule. These reforms ensure that concentrated wealth cannot be passed indefinitely through trust structures that avoid estate, gift, and generation-skipping taxes.

Estimated annual revenue: $15 to $25 billion.

## **4.6 Carried Interest Reclassification**

Fund manager compensation received as a percentage of investment profits (carried interest) is reclassified as ordinary income. Under current law, carried interest is taxed at the lower capital gains rate despite representing compensation for services. The Accord's capital gains reform (ordinary rates above $1M) partially addresses this, but explicit reclassification ensures no ambiguity remains.

Estimated annual revenue: $5 to $15 billion.

## **4.7 Charitable Remainder Trust Tightening**

Charitable remainder trusts used as tax shelters — where ultra-wealthy individuals donate appreciated assets, receive an immediate tax deduction, and then receive income from the trust for decades — are reformed to require minimum charitable distributions of at least 10% of trust assets annually and independent valuation of donated assets. This preserves legitimate charitable giving while eliminating the use of CRTs as tax avoidance vehicles.

Estimated annual revenue: $5 to $10 billion.

## **4.8 Statutory IRS Enforcement Floor**

The Internal Revenue Service receives a statutory funding floor of 0.5% of total federal revenue, automatically appropriated without annual Congressional action. At least 60% of enforcement resources above the current baseline must be directed toward returns above $500,000 and complex partnership structures.

The IRS gross tax gap is approximately $600 to $700 billion annually. The CBO and Treasury estimate that every $1 spent on enforcement of high-income returns generates $5 to $12 in additional revenue. IRS audit rates for millionaires fell from 8% to under 2% between 2010 and 2023 as the agency's budget was cut 20% in real terms. Restoring enforcement capacity and directing it toward high-income avoidance recovers revenue that is legally owed but uncollected.

Estimated annual revenue: $60 to $100 billion.

# **Part 5: The American Capital Fund (AMCF) — Governance and Structure**

## **5.1 Charter and Institutional Form**

The American Capital Fund (AMCF) is chartered as a government corporation modeled on the FDIC and TVA — institutions with operational independence, politically accountable leadership, and statutory mandates that constrain discretion without requiring full executive control.

* The AMCF is governed by a Board of Directors subject to Senate confirmation, ensuring political accountability without day-to-day political interference.

* Spending is governed by the statutory priority waterfall (see Part 7). Modifying waterfall priorities requires a supermajority vote of both chambers.

* The AMCF is a strictly passive investor. It holds equity and receives dividends. It exercises no voting rights on the corporations in which it holds stakes. Governance and worker voice are handled entirely through the codetermination architecture.

## **5.2 Investment Mandate**

The AMCF's equity holdings are governed by an indexed passive mandate with a 21% ownership cap. Scrip received from the EV Growth Tax is held as received and not actively traded. Once the AMCF reaches 21% of total US corporate equity (projected around Year 19), the EV Growth Tax ceases generating scrip — the AMCF's domestic holdings grow organically through market appreciation only, permanently locked at 21% ownership. This cap is structurally self-enforcing: at 21% ownership, the AMCF already captures 21% of all future value creation through appreciation, making additional scrip mathematically redundant.

* Cash Generation: The AMCF generates cash through dividends and proportional buyback participation (Section 11). Any company that returns capital to shareholders through any mechanism must include the AMCF proportionally to its ownership stake. This makes the AMCF indifferent to corporate payout policy while ensuring robust cash flow regardless of whether companies prefer dividends or buybacks.

* No Voting Rights: The AMCF exercises no voting rights on the corporations in which it holds stakes. Governance and worker voice are handled entirely through the codetermination architecture. The AMCF is a passive economic participant, not a governance actor. Because the AMCF's 21% equity stake carries no votes, the effective voting distribution in every company at equilibrium is: workers 21% of voting shares (21 out of 79 voting shares = 26.6% effective), private shareholders 58% (58 out of 79 = 73.4% effective). Private shareholders retain a clear operating majority. Workers hold a blocking minority — they can prevent any special resolution requiring 75% supermajority approval (mergers, charter amendments, dilutive share issuances) without worker support. This structural balance ensures that no major corporate restructuring can occur over the combined objection of the workforce, while day-to-day business operations remain under management and shareholder control.

### **One Share, One Vote Governance Standard**

All entities above $10 million in enterprise value must maintain a single share class with equal voting rights — one share, one vote. Dual-class share structures, supervoting shares, non-voting share classes, and any other mechanism that severs economic ownership from proportional governance rights are prohibited. Existing dual-class structures must convert to single-class within 5 years of the Accord's enactment. During the transition period, all Equity Excise shares (worker PSUs) and Growth Tax scrip (AMCF units) are issued in the highest-vote share class, ensuring that workers and the AMCF receive governance-equivalent shares from day one.

This mandate is non-negotiable for the Accord's governance architecture to function. Without it, a founder holding 10x supervoting shares can maintain unilateral corporate control despite owning a minority of economic equity — rendering the workers' 21% ownership stake and the AMCF's 21% economic stake governmentally powerless. The entire blocking minority mechanism depends on proportional voting. Dual-class structures circumvent it entirely.

The one-share-one-vote standard has broad support in the institutional investor community. The Council of Institutional Investors, most major pension funds, and multiple stock exchanges (Hong Kong and Singapore require dual-class sunset clauses) have formally advocated for equal voting rights. The policy logic: if a company benefits from the American market ecosystem, accesses American capital markets, and operates under the protection of American law, its governance must reflect the proportional economic ownership of all shareholders — founders, private investors, workers, and the American public alike.

**⚠ Red Team Defense —** *"This kills founder-led innovation" objection: Dual-class structures exist to insulate founders from short-term market pressure. The Accord's 0% CIT, simplified tax code, and $10M Growth Tax exemption already provide the most founder-friendly business environment in the developed world. The one-share-one-vote standard asks founders to share governance proportionally with the workers and public who co-own the enterprise — not to surrender control. At 58% private shareholder voting power, a founder with a significant equity stake retains enormous influence. What they lose is the ability to unilaterally override every other stakeholder — which is a governance distortion, not an innovation enabler. The companies that have performed best over the long term (Berkshire Hathaway excepted) have generally operated under equal voting structures. The evidence that dual-class structures produce superior long-term returns is weak and contested.*

*Fallback position if political negotiation requires compromise: a 7-year sunset clause on existing dual-class structures with immediate prohibition on new dual-class issuance for entities above $10M EV. During the sunset period, Equity Excise and Growth Tax shares are issued in the highest-vote class. This achieves the same endpoint on a longer timeline while respecting existing contractual arrangements.*

* International Diversification: The AMCF accumulates international exposure through two organic channels without selling US corporate equity. First, cash proceeds from Tier 2 Closely-Held Cash Elections (Section 1.4) may be deployed into international index funds rather than US equities, at the discretion of the AMCF Board subject to the passive mandate. Second, scrip received from US subsidiaries of foreign-parented companies (Section 2.6) — including entities like Toyota USA, Samsung Electronics America, Nestlé USA — inherently provides exposure to internationally managed enterprises operating on US soil. Combined, these channels are projected to produce 15 to 25% international portfolio exposure at maturity without requiring the politically sensitive step of selling American corporate equity to purchase foreign assets. This diversification provides countercyclical protection: global assets do not move in lockstep with US GDP, ensuring the AMCF retains value during domestic economic downturns when tax revenue also falls.

* Institutional Analogue: The Norwegian Government Pension Fund Global, adapted to the American constitutional structure. The AMCF does not engage in activist investing, proxy contests, or directional bets on individual companies.

This ensures citizen ownership income reflects genuine national progress rather than financial market performance alone.*

## **5.4 Citizen AMCF Grants — Universal Per Capita Ownership**

Every American citizen receives an annual AMCF unit grant, denominated in the fund's equity units. Grants are universal, equal per capita, and unconditional. **AMCF grants are unit distributions, not cash payments.** Citizens receive ownership shares in the AMCF — a claim on the fund's underlying equity portfolio — not a check. Units appreciate as the fund's holdings appreciate. Citizens may liquidate units for cash at any time (subject to vesting), but the default state is accumulation. This design forces compounding: citizens who do not need immediate cash leave their units untouched, building wealth automatically. The prebate ($5,000/person/year in cash, CPI-indexed annually) and the carbon dividend (80% of carbon tax revenue returned per capita in cash) provide the guaranteed income floor. The AMCF grant provides the wealth-building engine.

* Grant size: 65% of AMCF annual cash income, converted to unit grants at net asset value and divided equally among all citizens, with a statutory minimum of $500 per capita in unit value annually regardless of fund size. During Phase 1, when 65% of cash income is insufficient to fund the $500 minimum, the shortfall is funded through the SPV bridge (Section 1.4). The AMCF becomes self-funding for the $500 grant floor by approximately Year 10. As the AMCF compounds, grants scale: approximately $503 per capita by Year 5, $1,724 by Year 10, $4,421 by Year 15, $9,430 by Year 20, and continuing to grow as the fund's cash income (dividends plus buyback participation) expands.

* Adult accounts: Units vest on a 3-year staggered schedule (33% per year). Vested units may be held indefinitely, rolled into qualified retirement accounts, or redeemed (liquidated) at AMCF net asset value. Unvested units may not be redeemed.

* Custodial accounts: Units issued to minors are held in Treasury Direct custodial accounts until age 18, at which point the full accumulated portfolio transfers to the citizen's personal AMCF account. No parental access mechanism exists; the account belongs to the child. Custodial units cannot be liquidated before transfer — they are pure compounding vehicles.

* Universal lifecycle: A child born today accumulates 18 years of compounding AMCF grants before reaching adulthood. With the $500 per capita minimum in early years scaling to over $5,000 as the fund matures, the custodial account reaches approximately $44,000 at transfer (deterministic at 5% real return, identical across all quintiles) — replacing and improving upon the separate baby bond program, which is eliminated.

### **Liquidation Mechanics and Tax Treatment**

AMCF unit grants are **tax-free when received.** No income is recognized at the time of distribution. Units enter the citizen's account at a cost basis equal to NAV on the date of grant.

When a citizen redeems (liquidates) vested units, the **appreciation since receipt is taxable as ordinary income** under the two-rate system: 25% on appreciation that falls within the citizen's sub-$1M income bracket, 50% on appreciation that pushes total income above $1M. There is no separate capital gains rate — all income is income. This is consistent with the Accord's unified income tax treatment.

This structure creates a natural incentive to hold rather than sell. Every year units are held, appreciation compounds tax-free. Liquidation triggers the tax. The longer a citizen holds, the more wealth accumulates tax-deferred. This mild hold incentive is the mechanism by which the AMCF builds generational wealth rather than functioning as a second prebate.

### **Expected Liquidation Behavior**

Liquidation rates vary by income level and decline over time as the Accord's cash income sources (prebate, carbon dividend, PSU dividends) reduce the consumption pressure that drives unit sales. Modeled as:

`liquidation_rate(percentile, year) = base_rate(percentile) × pressure_decay(year)`

Where `base_rate` reflects structural consumption pressure (highest for lowest-income households, approaching zero for the wealthy) and `pressure_decay = 0.5 + 0.5 × e^(-year/15)` reflects the declining need to liquidate as Accord-provided cash income scales up. Representative rates:

At Year 1, a P10 household (average income $15,000) liquidates approximately 90% of their $500 grant ($450 in cash income). A P50 household liquidates approximately 55%. A P80 household liquidates approximately 15%. At Year 20, the same P10 household liquidates approximately 50% of their $9,430 grant ($4,715 in cash, retaining $4,715 in compounding units). The P50 household liquidates approximately 20%. The P80 household liquidates approximately 5%.

The income-side impact of AMCF grants — the cash actually received by households — is therefore substantially less than the headline grant per capita, particularly for upper brackets. The wealth-side impact — the accumulated units compounding in each citizen's account — is correspondingly greater. A P50 household that retains 80% of its Year 20 grant accumulates $7,544 in additional units that year alone, compounding indefinitely until liquidated.

* ACI inflation damper: When CPI exceeds 3%, vesting continues on schedule but redemptions are paused. Vested units held during the pause earn a guaranteed 7% annual credit, payable in additional units. This converts the inflation brake from a perceived confiscation into a high-yield savings mechanism. Hardship distributions for medical emergencies, eviction prevention, and funeral expenses are exempt from the pause.

**⚠ Red Team Defense —** *Index gaming risk: With a composite ACI rather than a single metric, manipulation requires simultaneously gaming five independently measured government statistics from different agencies. The ACI weighting can be reviewed on a 5-year cycle by the CRO evaluation division with public comment, ensuring the measured components continue to track the underlying concept they represent.*

**⚠ Red Team Defense —** *"This is the Swedish Meidner Plan and it will fail the same way" objection:*

*The Meidner Plan (1975-1991) is the canonical example of a sovereign equity accumulation scheme that failed politically. Rudolf Meidner proposed that Swedish companies issue 20% of annual profits to union-controlled "wage-earner funds" that would accumulate majority and eventually total ownership of Swedish corporations. It triggered the largest demonstration in Swedish history (75,000-100,000 marchers), was implemented in a crippled form in 1984, accumulated a trivial $3 billion, and was dissolved entirely in 1991. The Accord is designed as the structural negative image of every Meidner failure:*

*Individual accounts vs collective funds: The Meidner funds were controlled by union leadership. No individual worker had a personal stake, a visible balance, or an account they could see. The AMCF distributes equity units to 330 million individual citizen accounts. Every American watches their balance grow. Dismantling the AMCF requires confiscating 330 million personal accounts — politically equivalent to confiscating bank accounts. The Meidner funds were dissolved with one parliamentary vote because no individual Swede lost a personal asset.*

*Hard cap vs open-ended accumulation: The Meidner Plan had no ownership ceiling — it was explicitly designed to transfer 100% of corporate ownership to workers over 20-35 years. This made it existentially threatening to every business owner. The AMCF caps at 21%. Worker equity caps at 21%. Combined public ownership caps at 42% permanently. Private shareholders retain 58% forever. The trajectory has a visible, legislated endpoint — not a slippery slope toward total collectivization.*

*Employer benefit vs pure extraction: The Meidner levy was paid from profits in cash, reducing company operating capital. It offered employers nothing in return. The Accord eliminates the corporate income tax entirely — companies gain cash (the full CIT savings) while transferring equity (scrip that costs zero cash). Apple saves $15 billion per year in real money under the Accord. The Meidner Plan cost every Swedish company real money every year. The Accord fractures business opposition because most employers are net beneficiaries; the Meidner Plan unified every Swedish employer against it because every employer was unambiguously worse off.*

*Passive investment vs political control: The Meidner funds were designed to accumulate voting control of corporations — union leadership would eventually direct corporate strategy. This was the existential threat that mobilized opposition. The AMCF exercises zero voting rights. It collects dividends and participates in buybacks. Period. Worker governance operates through individual shareholder votes via the Stewardship Trust, not through union-controlled collective funds directing political power.*

*Universal constituency vs union exclusivity: The Meidner funds benefited organized labor — a minority of the workforce. The AMCF benefits every American citizen regardless of union membership or employment. The political coalition is 330 million people, not 2 million union members. This is the Social Security principle: universal programs are politically indestructible because dismantling them requires taking something from everyone.*

*Market framing vs socialist framing: Meidner explicitly described his plan as a mechanism for ending private capital ownership. The Accord is framed as citizen capitalism — co-ownership within a market system, with 0% corporate tax, simplified individual tax, and permanent private majority control. The vocabulary matters: "social infrastructure return" and "citizen-owner" operate in a fundamentally different political register than "wage-earner funds" and "collective ownership."*

*The Meidner Plan proved that collective ownership accumulation without individual stakes, without employer benefit, without a cap, and without market-compatible framing is politically fatal. The Accord embodies the opposite of each failed element. The comparison is superficial — the only shared feature is "government acquires corporate equity" — and the structural differences are load-bearing.*

# **Part 6: Worker Codetermination — The Two-Pocket Mandate**

The codetermination framework delivers worker economic power through property rights rather than regulatory mandates. Capital dictates behavior; the framework legally dilutes concentrated capital by transferring ownership to workers, creating enterprise co-governance without requiring adversarial collective bargaining structures.

## **6.1 Stratified Protocol by Enterprise Value**

| Tier | EV Threshold | Obligations |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Tier 1 | $0 – $10M (exempt) | Productivity-scaled employer contribution to Sectoral Wealth Fund: `$1,000 + min($4,000, (3-year avg revenue per employee ÷ $100,000) × $500)`, subject to affordability cap (contribution ≤ 50% of CIT savings per worker); statutory governance rights for 5+ employee firms |
| Tier 2 | $10M – $100M | EV Growth Tax via mandatory valuation reporting (LP Peg, QLE, or Federal Valuation Office appraisal); 21% Stewardship Trust voting bloc; Sectoral Wealth Fund contributions equivalent to 21% Phantom Equity obligation; Closely-Held Cash Election available for EV Growth Tax payment |
| Tier 3 | Venture / Unprofitable | EV pegged to highest LP-reported NAV on a common-stock-equivalent basis; Convertible Instrument Floor applies for unpriced capital; liability satisfied via private equity scrip |
| Tier 4 | $100M+ | Full Two-Pocket Mandate plus EV Growth Tax to AMCF |

## **6.1A Tier 1 — Productivity-Scaled Sectoral Fund Contribution**

The Tier 1 contribution replaces the flat $1,000 deposit with a formula that scales with the employer's productivity while protecting thin-margin businesses through an affordability cap:

`Contribution per worker = min(formula, 50% × CIT savings per worker)`

`where formula = $1,000 + min($4,000, (3-year rolling avg revenue per employee ÷ $100,000) × $500)`

The floor is $1,000 per worker per year. The formula ceiling is $5,000 (reached at $800,000 revenue per employee). The affordability cap ensures no employer's contribution exceeds half of their estimated CIT savings from the Accord's 0% corporate income tax — making every Tier 1 contribution sustainable by construction. Representative outcomes: a 5-person plumbing shop ($300K revenue per employee, 15% margin) contributes $2,500 per worker and saves $9,450 in CIT — net savings of $6,950. A 15-person restaurant ($133K revenue per employee, 5% margin) has its formula contribution of $1,667 capped at $700 (50% of $1,400 CIT savings). A 10-person retail shop ($80K revenue per employee, 3% margin) is capped at $252. The cap ensures that even the thinnest-margin employers benefit from the Accord's CIT abolition.

The Sectoral Wealth Fund earns 6% gross return with 3.5% annual dividend distribution to workers. A worker receiving $2,500 per year in contributions accumulates a fund balance of approximately $33,000 by Year 10 and $198,000 by Year 30, paying dividends of $1,153 and $6,917 respectively. The fund is fully portable — it follows the worker across employers and does not require a cashout event on departure.

### **Owner-Operator Equity Rollover**

Workers who leave employment to start a business may roll their Sectoral Wealth Fund balance into their new company as founder's equity — tax-free, using the same mechanics as a 401(k) rollover. A plumber who works 8 years at a Tier 1 employer accumulating approximately $25,000 in fund value can roll that balance into a new plumbing business as startup capital, maintaining the tax-deferred compounding. This preserves the trade-to-ownership pathway as a credible wealth-building alternative to mega-cap PSU accumulation: a trade worker who rolls over $25,000 into a business that grows to $3 million over 22 years builds wealth comparable to decades of Tier 3 PSU cashouts, while owning 100% of the enterprise they created.

## **6.1B Universal Worker Definition and Platform Employment**

### **Worker Definition for Equity Excise Purposes**

For purposes of the Equity Excise, PSU allocation, Sectoral Wealth Fund eligibility, and all codetermination provisions, "employee" means any individual performing labor for an entity, regardless of classification as an independent contractor, freelancer, temporary worker, or any other non-employee designation under current labor law. The Accord's worker equity framework does not adopt or defer to existing IRS or Department of Labor classification tests (ABC test, common-law test, economic reality test). If a person performs work and the entity compensates them for that work, the person is a worker for codetermination purposes.

This definition encompasses: full-time salaried employees, hourly workers, part-time workers, seasonal workers, gig economy workers performing labor through digital platforms (rideshare drivers, delivery couriers, on-demand service providers), staffing agency workers (allocated to the client entity where work is performed, not the staffing agency), and any individual receiving a 1099-NEC or 1099-K from an entity above the $10 million EV exemption threshold.

### **Proportional FTE Allocation**

All PSU allocations, Sectoral Fund contributions, and Phantom Equity allocations are proportional to hours worked. Full-time equivalent (FTE) is calculated as:

`FTE = annual hours worked ÷ 2,080`

A full-time employee working 40 hours/week receives 1.0 FTE allocation. A part-time worker at 20 hours/week receives 0.50 FTE. A gig worker logging 10 hours/week on a platform receives 0.25 FTE. PSU value, dividend payments, and cashout amounts are all scaled by the worker's FTE fraction.

Entities must report annual hours worked for every worker (as defined above) who performs more than 100 hours of labor for the entity in a calendar year. Platform companies already track hours with precision through their dispatch and GPS systems — compliance requires only that they report what they already measure. The 100-hour minimum threshold excludes truly occasional one-time engagements while capturing anyone with a meaningful ongoing relationship with the entity.

### **Platform Company Application**

Digital labor platforms — including but not limited to rideshare, delivery, on-demand services, freelance marketplaces, and any entity that algorithmically matches workers to tasks, sets or constrains pricing, and processes payment — are subject to the Equity Excise on the same terms as all other employers above the $10 million EV threshold. Their workers are included in the PSU pool at their FTE-adjusted allocation.

A platform with $25 billion in enterprise value and 7 million active workers (averaging 8 hours/week, or approximately 800,000 FTE equivalents) maintains a 21% worker equity pool of $5 billion allocated across 800,000 FTE — approximately $6,250 per FTE. A driver working 25 hours/week (0.625 FTE) holds approximately $3,900 in PSU value, earning $137/year in dividends. A driver working 40 hours/week holds approximately $6,250 and earns $219/year.

These numbers are individually modest but they are not zero — which is what every gig worker currently builds in equity at the platforms that profit from their labor. The PSU stake compounds as the platform's EV grows: at 7.5% annual appreciation, the 0.625 FTE driver's stake grows to approximately $5,600 after 5 years of appreciation, paying $196/year. Over a career of 30 years of part-time platform work, the accumulated sectoral fund balance and reinvested cashouts produce meaningful wealth where the current system produces none.

The deeper structural effect: the universal worker definition creates a financial incentive for platforms to rationalize their workforce. A platform that maintains 7 million casual workers dilutes its PSU pool across 800,000 FTE, producing $6,250 per FTE — too thin to retain talent. A platform that restructures toward 200,000 full-time employees concentrates the same $5 billion pool into $25,000 per worker — a genuine retention tool. The Accord does not mandate this restructuring, but the economics strongly favor it: concentrated full-time employment produces better worker outcomes, lower turnover, and higher per-worker equity value. The gig economy's labor arbitrage becomes less profitable when the Equity Excise applies regardless of classification.

### **Owner and Executive Treatment**

Business owners (sole proprietors, majority shareholders, managing partners) do not receive PSUs from the companies they own. Their equity interest IS their ownership — PSUs exist to give non-owner workers an ownership stake they would otherwise lack. An owner of a plumbing shop who also works in the business receives no PSU allocation from their own company, but their employee plumbers do.

Executives and C-suite officers who are employees (not majority owners) DO receive PSU allocations on the same per-headcount basis as all other workers. The CEO of Amazon receives the same fractional PSU allocation as the warehouse worker. The difference is that the CEO's PSU ($266,000) is trivial relative to their $30 million total compensation, while the warehouse worker's identical PSU is transformative relative to their $35,000 salary. This per-headcount equality is a deliberate design choice — the codetermination framework is egalitarian by construction. Salary-weighting PSUs would replicate the existing compensation hierarchy inside the equity pool, defeating the purpose.

Founders of companies below the $10 million EV exemption threshold are not affected by the Equity Excise at all — their companies are Tier 1, exempt from PSU issuance. Once the company crosses $10 million, the founder's ownership is diluted by the 4.2% annual Equity Excise on the same terms as all other shareholders. The founder does not receive PSUs to offset this dilution — the dilution IS the mechanism by which worker equity is created.

## **6.1C Care Economy Ownership Credit — Extending Worker Equity to Unpaid Labor**

The codetermination framework builds worker equity through formal employment relationships. But millions of Americans perform essential economic labor outside formal employment: raising children, caring for elderly or disabled family members, pursuing education, or unable to work due to disability. These contributions are economically real — unpaid family caregiving alone represents over $500 billion per year in market-equivalent labor — but invisible to the worker ownership pillar. A stay-at-home parent performing 60 hours per week of childcare accumulates zero worker equity while a DoorDash driver at 10 hours per week accumulates proportional PSU-equivalent deposits. This is incoherent. The Care Economy Ownership Credit (CEOC) extends the worker ownership pillar to anyone performing documented care or human capital work who is not accumulating equity through formal employment.

### **Qualifying Categories**

**Primary child caregivers:** One parent per household with a child under age 12 who is not employed more than 20 hours per week. Qualifies at 1.0 FTE equivalent. Documentation: tax filing status plus child's birth certificate — automatic, no application required beyond existing tax filings. Both parents may qualify at partial FTE if both work part-time and neither exceeds 20 hours.

**Family eldercare and disability caregivers:** Individuals providing primary care to a family member aged 65 or older, or to a family member with a documented disability, who would otherwise require institutional or professional care. FTE determined by documented care hours, capped at 1.0. Documentation: physician certification of care recipient's need plus caregiver attestation. This parallels existing Medicaid self-directed care and VA Caregiver Support Program documentation frameworks.

**SSDI recipients:** Automatic enrollment at 1.0 FTE equivalent regardless of whether they perform care work. Their disability documents their exclusion from formal employment. SSDI recipients additionally receive AMCF unit grants at 3x the standard citizen rate (funded through AMCF unit issuance — negligible dilution against the total fund) to partially compensate for the permanent exclusion from PSU accumulation and the additional costs associated with disability.

**Full-time students (ages 18-26):** Enrolled full-time in accredited higher education or registered apprenticeship programs. Qualifies at 0.5 FTE equivalent — recognizing that education is investment in human capital that benefits the broader economy. Documentation: enrollment verification already collected through FAFSA.

**Unemployed (bridging):** Workers between jobs continue receiving CEOC deposits at 0.5 FTE for up to 12 months of unemployment, then taper to zero. This prevents the worker ownership pillar from resetting every time someone transitions between positions. Documentation: state unemployment insurance enrollment.

### **Contribution Mechanics**

The CEOC provides federal deposits into the same Sectoral Wealth Fund used by Tier 1 employers. The contribution rate is a flat federal floor:

`CEOC deposit = $2,000/year × FTE equivalent`

A full-time primary caregiver: $2,000 per year. A part-time family caregiver at 0.5 FTE: $1,000. An SSDI recipient: $2,000. A full-time student: $1,000. An unemployed worker bridging between jobs: $1,000.

The fund compounds at 6% gross with 3.5% annual dividend distribution — identical to Tier 1 sectoral fund mechanics. The fund is fully portable and follows the individual across life transitions. A parent who spends 10 years as a primary caregiver accumulates approximately $26,000 in fund value, paying roughly $900 per year in dividends by Year 10. When the parent returns to formal employment, the CEOC fund balance continues compounding alongside whatever PSU or employer-contributed sectoral fund equity they accumulate through their new job.

### **Enhanced AMCF Multiplier for Care Economy Participants**

All CEOC-qualifying individuals (except unemployed workers in the bridging period, who are expected to return to employment) receive AMCF unit grants at 2x the standard citizen rate. SSDI recipients receive 3x. These enhanced grants are funded through AMCF unit issuance and represent negligible dilution against the total fund.

At Year 10, a stay-at-home parent receives: standard AMCF grant of $1,724 enhanced to $3,448 per year, plus CEOC sectoral fund dividend of approximately $900, plus the $5,000 prebate, plus $843 carbon dividend — a total of approximately $10,191 per year in guaranteed income and wealth building. By Year 20, the enhanced AMCF alone reaches $18,860 per year. This does not match a full-time Tier 3 worker's combined PSU income, but it is an order of magnitude better than what caregivers currently receive for their labor — nothing — and it builds compounding wealth for retirement.

## **6.2 The Two-Pocket Mandate (Tier 4\)**

**Pocket 1: The Stewardship Trust (Power)**

* A distinct class of shares carries 20% floating voting weight, permanent regardless of dilution.

* Economic value is zero — no dividends, cannot be sold or transferred. This is pure governance power, not an economic instrument.

* Shadow Board: selected workers with NDAs review confidential financials alongside Trust representatives, ensuring information parity with the board.

**Pocket 2: The Phantom Equity Fund (Wealth)**

* Phantom Stock Units (PSUs) equivalent to 20% of company valuation at equilibrium.

* Funded by a 4.2% annual Equity Excise Tax until the 21% cap is reached.

* Dividends: proportional cash payout when the company pays shareholders.

* Exit: company redeems PSUs at FMV in cash, capped at 5% of free cash flow annually. Excess redemptions via 5-year promissory note.

* Living system: when workers depart and cash out, PSUs return to the pool. The Excise Tax reactivates to replenish, aligning new workers' incentives with company growth.

## **6.3 Constitutional Defense**

*The Two-Pocket Mandate is drafted as a federal excise tax on the privilege of corporate operation, not as a regulatory labor mandate or property appropriation. Pennell v. City of San Jose (1988) establishes that economic regulation affecting property value is not automatically a Taking. Cedar Point Nursery (2021) risk is neutralized by the tax-incident framing: the employer is not compelled to admit anyone onto property; they are levied an excise whose satisfaction mechanism involves equity allocation.*

# **Part 7: Sovereign Equity — Government Employee and Congressional Compensation**

The principal-agent problem in government — citizens want growth and wellbeing, government employees have fixed salary incentives unconnected to outcomes — is addressed through a compensation architecture that gives every public servant a financial stake in national success using the same AMCF units that every citizen holds. The framework covers three levels: federal civil servants (Section 7.1), congressional representatives (Section 7.2), and approximately 20 million state and local employees (Section 7.3).

## **7.1 The Federal Compensation Stack**

Government employees in the Congressional Regulation Office, federal civil service, and other legislative branch agencies receive compensation in four components:

| Component | Instrument | Trigger | Target Behavior |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Base Salary | Cash at market rate | Employment | Stability and competence; competitive with private sector for specialized expertise |
| Federal Equity Units (FEUs) | AMCF units at 3x citizen grant rate | Annual, ACI-linked | Align agency outcomes with national wellbeing; destroys indifference to growth |
| Gainshare Dividend | Project bonus pool | Specific outcome metrics (speed, savings, accuracy) | Ship faster, cheaper, and more accurately; rewards measurable delivery |
| Innovation Royalty | Bayh-Dole extension to policy and software | Adoption of tool or protocol by external entities | Attract high-value technical and policy talent with IP upside |

* FEU vesting: 5-year staggered vesting (20% per year). Units held during the ACI inflation pause earn the same 7% guaranteed credit as citizen units.

* Non-compete structure: Senior CRO staff are subject to a 5-year cooling-off period prohibiting lobbying or advocacy before Congress or the CRO on matters they worked on. Incoming staff with lobbying backgrounds face a 3-year cooling-off period before joining. Competitive compensation is the structural deterrent; the non-compete is the behavioral backstop.

* Pay authority: CRO staff are exempt from standard GS pay scales. An independent compensation committee sets pay based on market benchmarks for equivalent private-sector expertise, funded through dedicated congressional appropriations rather than executive branch budget processes.

## **7.2 Congressional Compensation Reform**

Congressional representatives receive the same alignment incentives through Congressional Stock Units (CSUs) that replace the existing FERS pension for new members, while preserving base salary as the stable income component.

* Base salary: $250,000 indexed to inflation. The 27th Amendment constrains immediate compensation changes; base salary continuity satisfies constitutional requirements.

* Congressional Stock Units: In lieu of the pension, new members receive annual CSU grants denominated in AMCF units. CSUs vest only after the member leaves office, eliminating any in-office trading incentive. The 27th Amendment is satisfied because the grant does not vary current compensation — it replaces a future benefit with a future claim on national performance.

* Blind trust requirement: Members are prohibited from holding individual securities. Their only permitted investment vehicle is the AMCF unit portfolio (citizen grant account plus CSU account), held in a mandatory blind trust administered by Treasury Direct. This eliminates insider trading structurally rather than through disclosure requirements that have proven inadequate.

* ACI alignment: CSU value is indexed to ACI performance over the member's tenure. A Congress that presides over ACI deterioration — wages stagnant, housing less affordable, health outcomes worsening — receives a smaller CSU payout upon departure. A Congress that improves all five components receives a substantial exit package.

**⚠ Red Team Defense —** *Short-termism risk: CSU vesting only upon leaving office creates an incentive to maximize the index in the final term before retirement rather than across a full career. The 5-year moving average basis for CSU valuation — same methodology as the Alaska Permanent Fund dividend — prevents single-year gaming. A member who inflates the housing affordability metric through a policy that collapses three years later will see that reflected in their 5-year average at payout.*

## **7.3 State and Local Public Service Equity Units**

Approximately 20 million state and local government employees — teachers, police officers, firefighters, public hospital workers, state university staff, transit workers, municipal administrators — are structurally excluded from the codetermination framework because there is no private enterprise value to share, no equity to dilute, and no corporate governance structure to democratize. Without a dedicated mechanism, these workers receive the universal citizen grant but lack the employer equity stake that private-sector workers accumulate through Phantom Stock Units. This creates an unfair two-tier system where public servants who educate children, ensure public safety, and maintain civic infrastructure are systematically disadvantaged relative to their private-sector counterparts.

### **Public Service Equity Units (PSEUs)**

All state and local government employees receive Public Service Equity Units at 3x the citizen grant rate — identical to the Federal Equity Units (FEUs) that federal civil servants receive under Section 7.1. PSEUs are denominated in AMCF units, issued from Year 1, and vest on the same 5-year staggered schedule (20% per year).

* Funding mechanism: PSEUs are funded through the AMCF's unit issuance authority, not from any cash tranche or the discretionary allocation. Issuing additional AMCF units against the existing equity pool produces negligible dilution — 20 million employees at 3x the Year 1 citizen grant ($1,500 per employee) represents $30 billion in unit issuance against a $590 billion fund (0.005% dilution). Even at Year 10 scale, the dilution remains below 0.5% of total fund value. This is the same mechanism by which citizen grants are funded in Phase 1 — unit claims on a growing asset pool, not cash withdrawals.

* Early compounding advantage: Public employees who receive PSEU grants from Year 1 benefit from the same early-mover compounding that makes the AMCF's citizen custodial accounts transformative. A teacher who receives $1,500 in AMCF units in Year 1 holds units that compound at market rates for decades. The earlier the grant, the greater the terminal value — which is why immediate enrollment rather than deferred activation is essential.

### **State ACI Variant — Jurisdiction-Level Performance Indexing**

Federal employee FEUs are indexed to the national ACI. State and local PSEUs are indexed to a State Commonwealth Index (SCI) — a jurisdiction-level variant of the ACI that measures wellbeing outcomes within the employee's state or locality. The SCI uses the same five components as the national ACI (real wage growth, housing affordability, health span, social mobility, GDP per capita) but calculated from state-level BLS, Census, and CDC data sources.

The SCI multiplier adjusts the PSEU grant rate: a state that outperforms the national ACI average on its SCI sees its employees receive PSEUs at a rate above 3x. A state that underperforms sees the rate decline toward 2x but never below 2x — the floor ensures public employees are never penalized below a meaningful threshold for factors outside their control.

This creates genuine alignment between state employee compensation and state-level outcomes. A police officer in a city where public safety improves sees a higher PSEU payout. A teacher in a state where social mobility increases sees her AMCF account grow faster. The same principal-agent correction that FEUs achieve for federal employees is replicated at the state and local level.

### **Participation Structure — Conditional Federal Funding**

State and local participation is structured as a condition of receiving federal funding — modeled on the National Minimum Drinking Age Act (1984), which conditioned federal highway funding on states adopting a 21-year drinking age. The precedent under South Dakota v. Dole (1987) permits Congress to attach conditions to federal spending that are related to the federal interest, not unduly coercive, and clearly stated.

Any state that enrolls its public employees in the PSEU system receives its full allocation of federal funding under all existing and Accord-created programs (LVT revenue sharing, healthcare reform distributions, infrastructure spending). States that decline forfeit a defined percentage of discretionary federal transfers — sufficient to make non-participation financially irrational without crossing the coercion threshold that the Supreme Court identified in NFIB v. Sebelius (2012). The condition is clearly related to the Accord's federal interest in ensuring all American workers participate in the ownership economy regardless of employer type.

**⚠ Red Team Defense —** *Tenth Amendment challenge: The PSEU mandate is a spending condition, not a direct regulation of state employment. States are free to decline, but choosing to forgo federal funding that their residents have already paid for through VAT and LVT is politically untenable. The Dole framework permits this level of conditionality. The Sebelius coercion doctrine applies only when the federal government threatens to revoke existing funding that states have come to depend on — new Accord-created funding streams (LVT revenue sharing, healthcare distributions) do not carry the same reliance-based coercion risk because states have no pre-existing dependency on them.*

# **Part 7A: Non-Profit Organizations — Worker Equity and AMCF Participation**

Approximately 12 to 13 million Americans — roughly 10% of the private workforce — are employed by non-profit organizations: hospitals, universities, charities, religious institutions, social service agencies, arts organizations, and foundations. These workers are structurally excluded from the for-profit codetermination framework because there is no enterprise value, no equity, no shareholders, and no profit to distribute. Without a dedicated mechanism, a nurse at Kaiser Permanente (non-profit) builds zero worker equity while an identical nurse at HCA Healthcare (for-profit) accumulates PSUs worth hundreds of thousands of dollars. This is an indefensible disparity rooted in tax classification rather than economic reality.

The Accord addresses this through a tiered framework that calibrates obligations to an organization's genuine financial capacity. The core principle: non-profit status is a mission classification, not a wealth exemption. Organizations that genuinely operate on thin margins serving community missions are treated gently. Organizations that have accumulated billions in assets, generate substantial investment returns, and pay executives millions while their custodial staff builds zero equity — their economic reality is that of a Fortune 500 company, and they should participate in the ownership economy accordingly.

## **7A.1 The Non-Profit Capacity Test**

Non-profit organizations are classified into three tiers based on data reported on IRS Form 990 — total revenue, total expenses, net assets, and executive compensation. The metrics are:

* **Surplus ratio**: (total revenue minus total expenses) divided by total revenue. Economically equivalent to a profit margin.
* **Net assets**: total assets minus total liabilities, including endowment value. Economically equivalent to retained earnings plus investment portfolio.
* **Executive compensation**: the highest individual compensation reported on Form 990 Schedule J.

## **7A.2 Tier A — Community Non-Profits**

**Criteria:** surplus below 10% of revenue AND net assets below $10 million.

These are genuinely mission-driven organizations operating on thin margins — the local food bank, the community health clinic, the neighborhood arts organization, the small religious congregation.

* **Worker equity obligation:** $1,000 per worker per year deposited into the Non-Profit Worker Equity Fund (a pooled investment vehicle invested in the same diversified passive index as the AMCF). For organizations with fewer than 50 employees, this obligation is waived — their workers instead receive AMCF units at 2x the citizen grant rate through federal unit issuance, identical to the micro-organization provision for public employees.

* **AMCF Growth Assessment:** None. Community non-profits do not participate in the Growth Assessment.

## **7A.3 Tier B — Established Non-Profits**

**Criteria:** surplus at or above 10% of revenue OR net assets between $10 million and $1 billion (and no employee earning above $1 million).

These are mid-size organizations with real financial capacity — regional hospital systems, mid-tier universities, large social service organizations, established cultural institutions.

* **Worker equity obligation:** the same productivity-scaled formula as for-profit Tier 1 companies:

`Contribution per worker = $1,000 + min($4,000, (3-year avg revenue per employee ÷ $100,000) × $500)`

A regional hospital with $500 million in revenue and 5,000 employees ($100,000 per employee): $1,500 per worker per year. A mid-size university with $200 million in revenue and 2,000 employees: $1,500 per worker. All contributions flow to the Non-Profit Worker Equity Fund, which workers accumulate, receive dividends from, and cash out upon departure — identical mechanics to the Tier 1 Sectoral Wealth Fund.

* **AMCF Growth Assessment:** None. Tier B non-profits do not participate in the Growth Assessment.

## **7A.4 Tier C — Wealthy Non-Profits**

**Criteria:** net assets above $1 billion, OR surplus above 10% of revenue with net assets above $100 million, OR any employee earning above $1 million in total compensation. Meeting ANY of these triggers classifies the organization as Tier C.

The $1 million executive compensation trigger ensures that non-profit hospitals and institutions that maintain nominal thin margins while paying executives at for-profit levels are captured. An organization claiming community non-profit status while compensating its CEO at $5 million is operating with for-profit economic incentives and should bear for-profit ownership obligations.

This tier includes approximately 100 to 200 organizations nationally: the top 50 university endowments, the major non-profit hospital systems (Kaiser Permanente, Mayo Clinic, Cleveland Clinic, New York-Presbyterian), the largest religious institutions with substantial investment portfolios, and the mega-foundations. Combined, they employ approximately 2 to 3 million workers.

### **Worker Equity — Full Codetermination Equivalent**

Tier C non-profits fund worker equity at the full equivalent of the for-profit Equity Excise. The "enterprise value equivalent" for a non-profit is calculated as:

`EV equivalent = total net assets + (annual surplus × 10)`

The organization contributes to the Non-Profit Worker Equity Fund at a rate of 4.2% of EV equivalent annually, ramping to a 21% worker equity pool equivalent over 5 years — identical to the for-profit Equity Excise schedule. A per-worker cap of $500,000 in PSU-equivalent stake prevents extreme endowment-per-worker outliers (such as foundations with small staffs and enormous assets) from distorting the framework. Workers accumulate shares in the fund, receive dividends, and cash out at fair market value upon departure.

Example — Harvard University: $53 billion endowment + ($500 million surplus × 10) = $58 billion EV equivalent. Raw per-worker stake at 21% equilibrium: $609,000 (approximately 20,000 employees), capped at $500,000. At 3.5% dividend yield: $17,500 per year in worker equity dividends. Annualized cashout at departure: approximately $164,000.

Example — Kaiser Permanente: estimated $50 billion net assets + ($2 billion surplus × 10) = $70 billion EV equivalent. Per worker (300,000 employees): $46,700 in fund value at equilibrium — well below the cap. Dividends: $1,635 per year. Annualized cashout at departure: approximately $15,300.

Example — Gates Foundation: $60 billion EV equivalent with only 2,000 employees produces a raw per-worker stake of $6 million — capped at $500,000. After the cap, Gates Foundation employees receive the same $17,500 annual dividend and $164,000 annualized cashout as Harvard employees, preventing a single foundation from generating multi-million-dollar windfalls per worker.

### **AMCF Growth Assessment — The Social Infrastructure Return for Non-Profits**

The same social infrastructure return argument that justifies the EV Growth Tax on for-profit companies applies with full force to wealthy non-profits. Harvard's endowment grew from $37 billion to $53 billion over the last decade because of the American legal system protecting its assets, American financial markets generating returns, the American tax code granting complete tax exemption on investment gains, and American public funding for the basic research that Harvard commercializes. Kaiser generates $95 billion in annual revenue from operating in a healthcare market built on Medicare, Medicaid, publicly funded medical education, NIH research, and FDA drug approvals. Every wealthy non-profit is profiting from the same social infrastructure that for-profit companies are — they just are not paying for it.

The Growth Assessment applies to Tier C non-profits only and operates identically to the EV Growth Tax with a notional ownership cap to ensure parity with for-profit treatment:

**Phase 1 — Accumulation:** The non-profit pays 21% of annual net asset growth (excluding direct donations received) as a cash contribution to the AMCF. Each payment increases a Notional AMCF Account balance tracked for that organization. The notional balance also appreciates at the non-profit's own asset growth rate — because if this were real equity, it would be appreciating alongside the rest of the portfolio.

`Assessable Growth = max(0, Increase in Net Assets − Direct Donations Received)`
`Growth Assessment = Assessable Growth × 21%`
`Notional Balance = (Prior Balance × (1 + asset growth rate)) + Growth Assessment payment`
`Notional Ownership Pct = Notional Balance ÷ Total Net Assets`

The high-water mark applies identically to the for-profit Growth Tax. If net assets decline in a crash year, no assessment is owed. Assessment only triggers when net assets exceed the prior peak.

Direct donations are excluded from assessable growth because they represent voluntary charitable transfers that were already tax-deductible for the donor — the public already "paid" through the charitable deduction. Investment returns, operating surpluses, and unrealized gains are included because these are returns generated by operating in the American ecosystem.

**Phase 2 — Equilibrium (notional ownership reaches 21%):** The Growth Assessment stops permanently. No more cash payments on growth. Instead, the non-profit pays an annual Dividend Equivalent — a cash payment equal to the combined payout yield applied to the notional 21% stake:

`Dividend Equivalent = Total Net Assets × 21% × combined payout yield`

The combined payout yield ramps from 3.63% to 6% on the same schedule as the for-profit AMCF yield.

**This achieves exact parity with for-profit treatment.** During the accumulation phase, the non-profit transfers wealth to the AMCF at the same rate as a for-profit company issuing scrip. At equilibrium, the non-profit pays the AMCF at the same rate as a for-profit company paying dividends on a 21% ownership stake. The lifetime economic burden is identical regardless of tax classification. The only difference is the payment mechanism — cash instead of scrip — because there are no shares to issue. Tier C non-profits reach the 21% notional cap around Year 17-19, the same timeline as for-profit companies.

Example — Harvard at equilibrium (approximately Year 17): net assets approximately $120 billion. Dividend Equivalent: $120 billion × 21% × 6% = $1.51 billion per year. That is roughly 1.2% of Harvard's total net assets annually — a meaningful but completely sustainable payment from an endowment generating 7%+ returns. Harvard retains 75-80% of its investment income and all of its donated funds. The endowment continues growing. And Harvard's workers — the custodians, the administrative staff, the lab technicians, the dining hall workers — are building real wealth for the first time.

Example — LDS Church (Ensign Peak Advisors): estimated $100 billion+ in investment assets. Assuming $7 billion in annual investment returns and $5 billion in annual tithing donations: assessable growth = $7 billion (investment returns only, donations excluded). Phase 1 assessment: $1.4 billion per year. Notional cap reached in approximately Year 17-18. At equilibrium with $150 billion in net assets: Dividend Equivalent = $150 billion × 20% × 6% = $1.8 billion per year. The church retains all tithing income and 75-80% of investment returns. Its investment portfolio continues growing. The assessment falls entirely on the financial returns generated by operating in American markets — not on the religious mission, not on charitable giving, not on tithing.

**⚠ Red Team Defense —** *First Amendment challenge for religious institutions: The Growth Assessment does not tax religious practice, speech, or organizational structure. It assesses a share of investment returns on financial portfolios — activity that is economically identical to secular investing and constitutionally distinct from religious exercise. The LDS Church's Ensign Peak Advisors is functionally indistinguishable from a hedge fund. Taxing its investment returns implicates the Free Exercise Clause no more than taxing a church employee's salary does, which the government already does without constitutional objection. The assessment is facially neutral, generally applicable, and does not target religion — it applies to all Tier C non-profits regardless of religious affiliation.*

**⚠ Red Team Defense —** *"This will destroy charitable giving" objection: Direct donations are explicitly excluded from assessable growth. A donor who gives $10 million to Harvard generates zero additional Growth Assessment. The assessment falls on investment returns — income the non-profit generates from its existing assets, not from charitable intent. Charitable incentives are fully preserved. The only behavioral change the assessment might produce is pressure on wealthy non-profits to deploy more of their investment returns toward mission-related spending rather than accumulating ever-larger endowments — which is precisely the behavior that non-profit status was designed to encourage in the first place.*

# **Part 8: Immigration Stabilization — The Abundance Floor Framework**

The old-age dependency ratio is a primary structural driver of Medicare solvency and Social Security sustainability. Stabilizing the ratio through targeted immigration policy is a fiscal mechanism, not merely a labor market policy. The following framework addresses both the demographic and the economic dimensions simultaneously.

## **8.1 The Abundance Floor Act**

A statutory floor on annual visa issuance tied to the old-age dependency ratio. Congress mandates that enough working-age immigrants are admitted to maintain at least a 2.8 workers per retiree ratio. The visa cap rises automatically when native birth rates decline and retiree numbers grow. The Executive cannot reduce immigration below this floor without congressional action.

* Trigger: Set at 3.2 workers per retiree to build the pipeline before the demographic cliff rather than responding to it. The 2.8 floor is the emergency threshold; policy activates proactively at 3.2.

* Fiscal mechanism: Dependency ratio improvement reduces the Medicare funding gap by approximately 0.3 to 0.5 percentage points of payroll over the long run. Combined with the Healthcare Reserve, this addresses the demographic and cost dimensions simultaneously.

## **8.2 Complementary Visa Categories**

# **Part 9: The National Housing Corporation — The Third Pillar of Ownership**

The Accord's ownership thesis stands on three legs: corporate ownership through the AMCF, worker ownership through codetermination, and home ownership through the National Housing Corporation. Without the third leg, the first two build financial wealth that gets extracted through housing costs. The AMCF generates $25,924 per person in annual unit grants by Year 30; if rents absorb that wealth through speculative land pricing, the Accord has built a more elaborate pipeline from workers to landlords rather than a genuine ownership economy.

The NHC is a congressionally chartered, ring-fenced public corporation — structurally closer to the TVA or the Federal Reserve than to HUD or a line agency. It acquires land, develops mixed-income housing at scale, retains the land permanently through a national Community Land Trust framework, and operates on a self-sustaining parallel balance sheet backed by rental and commercial revenue. It is an infrastructure institution, not a welfare program.

## **9.1 Institutional Design**

The NHC is capitalized with an initial federal endowment and authorized to issue its own bonds backed by rental income streams. NHC bonds are not sovereign debt — they do not appear on the federal balance sheet, do not count toward gross federal debt, and do not affect the Accord's fiscal trajectory. This is the TVA model: a public corporation with its own creditworthiness derived from its own revenue.

Rental income and commercial revenue flow back into the NHC's development fund rather than to general revenue. A mandatory capital reserve fund — calculated actuarially based on lifecycle maintenance costs of the building stock — is set aside from all revenue before any other expenditure. This reserve is written into the enabling legislation, not left to administrative discretion. This is where American public housing failed most specifically: maintenance budgets were raided through annual appropriations politics until buildings became uninhabitable. The NHC's ring-fenced structure makes that failure mode structurally impossible.

Post-debt surplus earmark: once the Accord's gross federal debt is eliminated (approximately Year 28-30), a minimum of 1% of the annual general fund surplus is earmarked to the NHC development fund. At Year 30 surplus levels (~$8.88 trillion), this represents approximately $89 billion annually — permanently eliminating any financing constraint on the NHC's mission.

## **9.2 Land Acquisition Hierarchy**

Permanent land retention through the CLT framework is non-negotiable. Every unit of land acquired by the NHC is held permanently — never resold into the speculative market. This is the single design choice that determines whether affordability is structural and permanent or a one-time intervention that reprices to market within a generation. Every Vienna Gemeindebau unit built in the 1920s is still affordable because the land was never re-entered into speculation.

Land acquisition follows a strict hierarchy designed to minimize political controversy and cost:

**Tier 1 — Public land transfers.** Federal surplus land (BRAC-closed military installations, underutilized federal campuses, USPS properties, HUD REO portfolio, surplus parking facilities), state and municipal surplus land (surface parking lots, defunct facilities, tax-reverted parcels), and transit authority land (surplus parcels around stations, maintenance yards, buffer land). The NHC receives right of first refusal on all federal surplus property disposals before public auction. Cost: near zero. Political controversy: minimal.

**Tier 2 — LVT-induced willing sellers.** The Accord's 10% LVT imposes an ongoing carrying cost on land proportional to its unimproved value regardless of whether it is developed. For landowners holding underutilized parcels in anticipation of speculative appreciation, LVT makes the hold strategy progressively more expensive. The NHC acquires from motivated sellers at LVT-suppressed prices through negotiated purchase — voluntary transactions at structurally fair prices reflecting productive-use value rather than speculative-hold value. This pipeline strengthens over Years 5-15 as the carrying cost burden accumulates on holdouts.

**Tier 3 — Tax-delinquent land.** Municipalities transfer their tax-delinquent parcel inventories to the NHC in exchange for NHC development capital and expertise. The municipality converts a legal liability into developed, revenue-generating property. The NHC acquires land at essentially zero cost.

**Tier 4 — Negotiated upzone agreements.** The NHC approaches private landowners with an offer: the NHC supports upzoning their parcel to high-density mixed use (dramatically increasing its value) in exchange for transferring 40-50% of the land area to the NHC at pre-upzone prices. Both parties benefit — the landowner captures upzone value on retained land, the NHC acquires below-market. No eminent domain, genuinely voluntary, and the NHC is creating value rather than extracting it.

**Tier 5 — Brownfield remediation sites.** EPA Superfund remediation linked programmatically to NHC development priority. Environmental contamination makes land cheap but development-prohibitive for private developers. The federal government already has remediation capacity. Clean it up, transfer to NHC, develop. This converts environmental liabilities into community assets in exactly the urban-core locations where NHC development does the most good.

**Tier 6 — Eminent domain (last resort, narrow authority).** Limited to strategic gap parcels (a single holdout blocking an otherwise assembled site) and long-term vacant blighted property. Bright lines written into enabling legislation: no eminent domain against owner-occupied primary residences, no eminent domain against operating small businesses, mandatory 150% of appraised value compensation. The NHC cannot afford a Kelo v. New London moment.

Blended land acquisition cost across all tiers: approximately 10-15% of per-unit development cost, versus 30-60% for private developers in high-demand markets.

## **9.3 LVT Integration and the Value Capture Loop**

The LVT synergy is the Accord's most direct integration point. LVT suppresses land speculation, which compresses NHC acquisition costs. The NHC retains land permanently through the CLT, meaning the LVT's anti-speculation logic is locked in institutionally. The two instruments reinforce each other's permanence.

The value capture loop operates as follows: public transit investment raises surrounding land values. LVT captures that increase as revenue. That revenue is available to the NHC's development fund. The NHC develops transit-oriented housing, which adds residents. More residents generate more transit ridership, which justifies further transit investment. The cycle is self-reinforcing.

Every NHC development above a minimum density threshold is sited within walkable distance of high-frequency transit. Site selection explicitly prioritizes transit corridors and planned transit extensions — building ahead of transit where the land acquisition cost advantage is largest.

LVT revenue enhancement from NHC development: estimated $5-15 billion per year at scale as NHC sites increase surrounding land values. This revenue flows through the Accord's general fiscal trajectory and modestly accelerates fiscal crossover.

## **9.4 Development Standards — Form-Based Design Code**

The NHC adopts form-based codes rather than use-based zoning as its design framework. Form-based codes regulate how buildings relate to streets, facades, massing, ground-floor activation, and materiality — producing the built environments people actually enjoy — while remaining indifferent to specific uses.

Non-negotiable design requirements for all NHC developments:

**Unit composition mandate:** No less than 60% of units in any development are 2-bedroom or larger. No less than 25% are 3-bedroom or larger. This is a design constraint, not a target. The private market systematically underproduces family-sized units because studios and 1-bedrooms maximize revenue per square foot. The NHC corrects this structural gap.

**Building typology:** Default to typologies that produce genuinely livable large units — perimeter blocks (units face outward onto streets and courtyards), single-loaded corridors (cross-ventilation, natural light on two sides), and townhouse rows for ground-floor family units with direct outdoor access. Double-loaded corridor apartment buildings — the default American multifamily type — are prohibited as the primary typology because they make large units dark, poorly ventilated, and acoustically inadequate for families.

**Build-to lines** rather than setbacks — buildings meet the street, creating the spatial enclosure that makes streets feel like places. **Ground floor activation requirements** — minimum percentage of ground-floor frontage must be transparent and occupied by uses generating foot traffic. **Facade articulation standards** preventing flat, featureless walls. **Material quality floors** prohibiting the cheapest cladding materials that signal impermanence. **Parking behind or under buildings** rather than between building and street.

**Community design:** Courtyards, shared outdoor space scaled for children, ground-floor units with private outdoor access for families. These draw from the Wiener Hof model where the building itself creates semi-private community space that produces social cohesion.

**Local aesthetic collaboration:** The NHC works with local municipalities on aesthetic character. "Tell us what your community looks like at its best and we'll build to that" rather than imposing a standardized federal product. This converts potential NIMBY opponents into partial co-owners of design outcomes. Federal preemption on zoning becomes less politically toxic when the aesthetic conversation is genuinely collaborative.

## **9.5 Mixed Use and Mixed Income**

**Mixed income is not optional.** Income-capped housing reliably produces concentrated poverty, social stigma, and the political isolation that leads to maintenance underfunding. NHC developments are genuinely mixed income, with rents at market rates across a wide band — from households using their prebate as primary income up through working families at 120-150% of Area Median Income. Higher-income residents cross-subsidize lower-income residents slightly while anchoring social mix and political legitimacy. This is Vienna's model: professionals live alongside lower-income households, and the program maintains middle-class political support because middle-class households participate in it.

**Rents are at market rates.** The NHC is a supply intervention, not a demand subsidy. The Accord's prebate ($5,000/person, CPI-indexed), carbon dividend ($843/person), and growing AMCF liquidation income provide the income floor that makes market-rate rents affordable for lower-income households without requiring a separate housing subsidy bureaucracy. A family of four receiving $20,000+ in annual prebate income plus employment income can afford NHC market rents. If rents are still unaffordable after the Accord's transfers, the problem is insufficient NHC supply — the solution is more units, not rent subsidies that mask the supply failure.

No income maximum is set low enough to exclude working families with children — the demographic the NHC is designed to serve.

**Mixed use is mandatory** above a minimum density threshold. Ground floor commercial in every qualifying development: childcare centers, medical clinics, grocery, restaurants, cafes, markets, independent retail. Ground-floor commercial tenants are recruited as genuine destinations, not just convenience services. Below-market commercial rents attract quality operators initially, with the bet that successful commercial activation increases the overall development's value. Childcare in particular has a direct fertility-enabling effect and is a logical fit for developments designed for family formation.

## **9.6 CLT Ownership Track — AMCF as Down Payment**

For ownership-track units, the CLT model separates land ownership (NHC retains permanently) from structure ownership (household purchases). Stripping land cost from the purchase price reduces it by 30-60% in high-demand markets, making homeownership accessible at income levels where it is currently impossible.

The AMCF custodial account ($44,000 at age 18) becomes a realistic down payment on a CLT structure. An 18-year-old's AMCF account, growing at 5% real, reaches approximately $72,000 by age 25 — sufficient for a 20% down payment on a $360,000 CLT structure (which, with land cost stripped, represents a unit that would cost $550,000-$700,000 on the private market in a mid-tier city). This is the ownership pathway integration point: the AMCF builds the capital, the NHC/CLT provides the accessible price point, and the LVT suppresses the speculative land inflation that would otherwise erode both.

**LVT treatment for CLT homeowners:** The 10% LVT does not apply to CLT ownership-track structures because the land is publicly held by the NHC. The homeowner owns only the structure, which is not subject to LVT (LVT applies to land value, not improvement value). The $500,000 homeowner exemption in the Accord's LVT framework is therefore not consumed by CLT ownership, preserving it if the household later purchases land-inclusive property.

## **9.7 Scale Commitment**

The US housing stock is approximately 145 million units. The existing shortage is estimated at 3.8-7 million units. The NHC's target is 4-6 million units over 15-20 years — approximately 250,000-400,000 units per year at peak production. Combined with LVT-induced private development (estimated to contribute 60-70% of total needed supply), this produces a 10-15% increase in total housing stock sufficient to measurably moderate rent growth in constrained markets.

Total capital commitment: approximately $1-1.5 trillion over 20 years at a blended per-unit cost of $255,000-$265,000 (including 10-15% land acquisition cost on the fraction of units requiring negotiated purchase). Annual capital deployment at peak: $60-100 billion.

**Self-sustainability:** A 3BR unit in a mid-tier market at $1,600/month market rent produces 8.3% gross yield on $230,000 construction cost. Net of operating costs (35% of revenue), net operating income yields approximately 5.4%. Against NHC bond financing at treasury-adjacent rates (4-4.5%), positive carry is 1-1.5% before commercial income. With ground-floor commercial revenue, positive carry reaches 2-3%. The NHC is self-sustaining at the development level under reasonable assumptions without any ongoing federal appropriation.

**Private development influence thresholds:**

| Threshold | Scale | Timeline | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Demonstration | 50,000-100,000 units | Years 1-3 | Changes aesthetic and design conversation; NHC projects become reference point |
| Competition | 500,000-1,000,000 units | Years 3-8 | Private developers must improve quality to compete; LVT suppresses land banking |
| Price influence | 2-3 million units | Years 8-15 | Visible rent moderation; sustains political coalition for continued investment |

The enabling legislation must commit to reaching the Competition threshold on a defined timeline, not leave scale to annual appropriations discretion. Below that threshold, the NHC risks being a boutique program that produces good outcomes for a small number of households without changing market dynamics.

## **9.8 Workforce Development Integration**

The binding constraint on NHC production is not capital but construction workforce capacity. The US construction industry employs 7-8 million workers. Doubling multifamily production at peak requires 20-30% workforce expansion in relevant trades.

The NHC pairs with explicit construction workforce development: apprenticeship expansion (integrated with the Accord's Abundance Floor immigration framework for construction trade skills), trade school investment, and deliberate geographic distribution of early NHC projects to build local construction capacity in lower-cost markets before tackling high-cost coastal metros.

The codetermination framework directly supports workforce retention. Construction workers accumulating PSU equity in the firms doing NHC work have a stake in the industry's expansion. Experienced tradespeople stay rather than cycling out, compounding capacity over time. The Tier 1 sectoral fund's productivity-scaled contribution ($1,000-$5,000/worker/year) provides additional wealth accumulation for construction workers at smaller specialty subcontractors.

## **9.9 ACI Integration**

A child born in the bottom quintile currently has roughly a 7% chance of becoming a patent-holding inventor versus 30% for a top-quintile child (Chetty, Hurst, Bell 2018). The gap is access, not talent. The Accord's $44,000 custodial account, universal prebate, NHC housing access, and PSU wealth accumulation dramatically expand the pool of potential innovators. If even a fraction of the bottom 50%'s suppressed innovative capacity is unlocked, the growth effect compounds indefinitely because ideas are non-rival.

**Channel 4 — Financial stability.** The AMCF's market stabilization effect (reducing 2008-type crash depth by 10.7 percentage points and recovery time by 13 months) prevents the GDP destruction caused by financial crises. The 2008 crisis permanently reduced US GDP by an estimated 5-10% relative to pre-crisis trend. If the AMCF prevents or reduces even one comparable crisis over 30 years, the cumulative growth benefit is measured in trillions.

**Channel 5 — Institutional simplification.** The two-rate income tax with zero deductions eliminates approximately $400-500 billion annually in compliance costs — money currently spent on tax preparation, planning, and avoidance that produces zero economic value. The 0% CIT eliminates corporate tax planning entirely. This is equivalent to a permanent 1.5-2% of GDP redirected from deadweight compliance activity to productive use.

### **Growth Capture Is Not Growth Reduction**

A critical conceptual distinction: the Accord captures a share of economic growth for broad distribution. It does not reduce the growth itself. GDP measures total economic output — goods produced, services delivered, transactions completed. The AMCF's 21% ownership of a company does not cause that company to produce fewer products, serve fewer customers, or employ fewer workers. The Equity Excise's 21% worker stake does not reduce the company's revenue or output. The factory produces the same number of cars regardless of whether the shareholders are three billionaires or three billionaires plus 50,000 workers plus 330 million citizen-owners. Output is identical. Only the distribution of returns on that output changes.

The Growth Tax is not a toll on the road that slows traffic. It is a split in the river that irrigates more fields. The same volume of water flows. The same economic output is generated. The returns are simply distributed more broadly — through AMCF unit grants, PSU dividends, PSU cashouts, and the prebate — rather than concentrating exclusively among existing shareholders.

And the macroeconomic evidence from the five channels above suggests the redistribution actually *accelerates* growth. A company whose workers own 21% has lower turnover and higher productivity (the German codetermination literature consistently demonstrates this). A consumer base receiving $5,000/person in prebate income and growing AMCF grants has more spending power, generating more demand for the company's products. A society with broader access to education and capital produces more innovators. The growth capture and the growth acceleration are complementary, not in tension — the Accord both grows the pie faster and distributes it more broadly.

### **Inflation Analysis**

The Accord creates both inflationary and deflationary pressures. The net effect is a manageable one-time price level adjustment followed by structural inflation at or near baseline rates.

**One-time price level shift (Year 1-2, then stabilizes):** The 4% VAT raises consumer prices by approximately 4%. The $100/ton carbon tax raises energy and transportation costs. Combined, the one-time price level adjustment is approximately 4-6%. This is not sustained inflation — it is a single adjustment after which prices stabilize at the new level. The prebate fully offsets the VAT on $125,000 of annual consumption (covering ~80% of households). The carbon dividend (80% of carbon revenue returned per capita) offsets the carbon cost increase for lower-income households. Federal Reserve monetary policy should treat these as transitory supply-side price adjustments, not demand-driven inflation requiring tightening.

**Velocity-driven demand pressure (Years 2-10, mild and absorbed):** Shifting income from low-MPC to high-MPC households increases the velocity of money — more transactions per dollar per year — creating approximately 0.3-0.5 percentage points of additional inflationary pressure above baseline. This is absorbed over 5-10 years as the supply side expands: 0% CIT-driven corporate investment increases productive capacity, NHC housing supply moderates rent inflation, and LVT suppresses the speculative land price appreciation that is historically one of the primary drivers of cost-of-living increases.

**Deflationary structural forces (Years 5-30):** The LVT is explicitly deflationary for land and housing — it suppresses speculative holding, reducing the asset price inflation that has driven cost-of-living increases for decades. The NHC's 4-6 million new units are a massive supply expansion in the most inflation-prone sector. The carbon tax is self-liquidating as decarbonization proceeds — its inflationary contribution declines toward zero over 15-20 years. The 0% CIT increases corporate investment capacity, expanding the supply of goods and services that absorbs the increased consumer demand.

**Built-in automatic stabilizers:** The ACI inflation damper pauses AMCF unit redemptions when CPI exceeds 3%, automatically reducing the cash flowing from AMCF liquidation into consumer spending without requiring Fed action or political decisions. The prebate is CPI-indexed but adjusts with a lag — if inflation spikes, the prebate's real value temporarily declines, which is automatically contractionary (the opposite of a wage-price spiral). The AMCF unit grants are wealth, not cash — they do not enter circulation unless citizens choose to liquidate, and the liquidation rate model shows declining rates over time as cash income sources scale up.

**Net assessment:** The Accord produces a one-time 4-6% price level adjustment in Year 1-2, followed by 0.3-0.5pp of mild additional inflation for Years 2-10 as the velocity increase works through the economy, followed by convergence to baseline or slightly below-baseline inflation as the supply-side expansions (NHC, 0% CIT investment, carbon tax self-liquidation, LVT land price suppression) fully absorb the demand increase. The fiscal trajectory simulation accounts for the Year 1-2 transition friction. The long-run inflation trajectory is structurally sound because the Accord does not print money — it redistributes existing income through the tax system, which changes who spends but not how much money exists.

**⚠ Red Team Defense —** *"This will cause runaway inflation" objection: The Accord does not expand the money supply. It redistributes existing income from low-velocity savers to high-velocity spenders. In monetary terms (MV = PQ), V increases but M does not — and the supply side (Q) expands through 0% CIT investment, NHC housing, and LVT-driven development, absorbing the velocity increase as real output growth rather than price increases. The one-time VAT and carbon tax price adjustments are supply-side level shifts, not demand-driven inflation spirals. The ACI inflation damper provides an automatic brake if CPI exceeds 3%. The prebate replaces dissolved welfare programs dollar-for-dollar and is CPI-indexed to maintain purchasing power — it does not create new spending capacity beyond what the dissolved programs already provided. The incremental spending capacity comes from AMCF liquidation and PSU dividends, which scale gradually over 10-20 years rather than arriving as a demand shock.*

# **Part 13: Microeconomic Stress Tests**

## **The Lower 25%**

The prebate functions as an unconditional negative income tax. A single adult below the poverty line receives $5,000 annually (CPI-indexed, growing with inflation) regardless of employment status. A household of three receives $15,000. The welfare cliff is eliminated: a wage increase no longer triggers loss of benefits. The LVT-driven housing supply expansion reduces local rent pressure over 5 to 10 years. The VAT increases consumption costs, but the prebate is calibrated to fully offset the VAT burden at poverty-level consumption — the lowest-income households pay zero net consumption tax.

Validated distributional simulation confirms: at Year 1, the bottom 10% of households see annual income rise from $15,000 to $21,000 — a 46% increase driven by the prebate, carbon dividend, and dissolved welfare replacement. Households at the 10th-20th percentile see income rise from $28,000 to $43,000 (+52%). These are not abstract projections; they reflect the prebate ($5,000/person in cash), carbon dividend ($843/person in cash), AMCF unit grants (partially liquidated), PSU dividends, and the net tax change operating simultaneously. By Year 30, the bottom 10% earn $55,000 annually — a 224% increase over current law — and the 10th-20th percentile earns $100,000, above today's median household income.

This is the most dramatic wealth transformation in the entire Accord: the bottom 20% goes from systemic poverty risk to guaranteed economic security.

## **Market Stabilization**

The AMCF at its 21% ownership cap functions as a permanent non-trading stabilizer in US equity markets. Validated simulation of historical crash scenarios shows measurable but modest improvement:

The 2008 Financial Crisis: crash depth reduces from 56.6% to approximately 45.9% (10.7 percentage point improvement) at 15% AMCF ownership (~Year 12+) with recovery shortened by approximately 13 months (66 to 53 months). The 2020 COVID Crash: depth reduces from 34.0% to approximately 28.9% (5.1 percentage points) with recovery unchanged at approximately 6 months. The 2022 Bear Market: depth reduces from 25.5% to approximately 21.9% (3.6 percentage points) with recovery shortened by approximately 2 months (24 to 22 months).

The AMCF does not prevent crashes — it reduces worst-case severity and shortens recovery by removing 20% of potential forced-selling volume from the market during drawdowns. The stabilization effect is largest during liquidity-driven crises (2008) where forced selling through margin calls and fund redemptions drives the cascade, and smallest during short, sharp corrections (2020 COVID) where recovery happens before the stabilization mechanism has time to differentiate.

# **Conclusion: The Philosophical Spine**

The American Ownership Accord is not a collection of programs. It is a unified theory of what went wrong in American political economy and what the corrective is.

The diagnosis: the American founding contained a genuine commitment to distributed power — economic, political, and institutional — that was progressively undermined. The concentration of capital in the Gilded Age destroyed the Jeffersonian vision of a republic of property-owning citizens. The administrative state's delegated regulatory power created accountability gaps that both parties have exploited. The two-party duopoly produced legislative capture by precisely the interests that comprehensive reform threatens.

The prescription: every American should be a capital owner from birth. The ownership deepens through working life. The government should hold capital on behalf of citizens, not merely redistribute income between them. And national progress should be measurable, visible, and financially connected to every citizen's personal economic position — not through the opacity of GDP statistics but through the tangibility of an account balance that goes up when wages grow and housing becomes more affordable.

The Accord does not redistribute wealth. It establishes co-ownership. The American market ecosystem — 330 million consumers, the world's deepest capital markets, a legal system that enforces contracts, a workforce educated at public expense, an infrastructure network built over two centuries — creates enormous value. Under the current system, 100% of that value accrues to private shareholders, most of whom are already wealthy. The Accord recognizes that the ecosystem is a collective creation and ensures that 20% of the value it generates flows back to the people who constitute it. This is not taking from one group to give to another. It is returning to every citizen a share of what their collective participation built.

This is not socialism. It is the oldest argument in American political thought, updated. The founding promise — that this would be a republic of free and equal citizens with genuine economic and political agency — was real, and it was undermined. The Accord is the project of redeeming it.
